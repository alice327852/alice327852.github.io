Title: AITA for letting my stepbrother eat my “last meal” knowing it would cost him millions?

The smell of beef Wellington is a ghost. It’s the scent of special occasions, of my mother’s proud smile, of a warmth that vanished from our house the day she died. For me, it’s also the smell of a locked door, a secret, and a last wish.

My mom, Eleanor, was a culinary genius in our eyes. Not a famous chef, but the kind who could make a Tuesday night feel like a feast. Her family, the Calloways, had old money, the quiet kind that’s been tucked away in trusts and investments for generations. My dad, a decent but easily swayed man, married her after college. My stepbrother, Chad (yes, really), and his mother, Brenda, arrived on the scene five years ago. Brenda married my dad within a year of Mom’s diagnosis. Convenient.

Mom saw through them immediately. She was weak from the chemo, but her eyes were sharp. A week before she passed, she called me to her bedside, her voice a papery whisper. She handed me a simple, leather-bound notebook, its pages stained with decades of butter and memories.

“This is everything, Leo,” she said. “But it’s also a test. My final one. Master the Wellington. It’s on page forty-seven. It’s… important.”

I promised. I hugged the book like a holy text. Inside, in her looping script, were recipes for everything from perfect pancakes to a coq au vin that could make you cry. And on page forty-seven, “Eleanor’s Ultimate Beef Wellington,” with notes in the margins like “puff pastry must be cold as a liar’s handshake” and “the duxelles should sing of earth and thyme.”

Her will was read a month after the funeral. The reading was to be followed by a “family dinner,” a tradition Dad insisted on, though the family now included Brenda and Chad, who’d already started eyeing the art on the walls. Chad, a gym-rat with the emotional depth of a teaspoon and a notorious, unrepentant food thief, had eaten leftovers labeled with my name since the day he moved in. Complaining only made Dad sigh about “blending the family.”

The day of the reading, I knew what I had to do. I woke up at dawn. I prepared the duxelles, sweating down mushrooms, shallots, and thyme until they were a rich, fragrant paste. I seared the… centerpiece. I wrapped it in the duxelles, then in a layer of impossibly thin crepes, then finally encased it all in buttery, golden puff pastry. I egg-washed it to a sheen and slid it into the oven. For two hours, the kitchen filled with a perfume so potent, so evocative of my mother, it felt like she was standing beside me.

When it emerged, it was a masterpiece. A golden-brown parcel of pure love and memory. I let it rest on the counter, as the recipe dictated, the most beautiful thing I’d ever created. I went upstairs to change, my heart pounding with a mix of pride and grief.

I came down to a scene I’d predicted, yet still felt like a punch to the gut. Chad stood by the empty baking tray, his mouth greasy, crumbs of puff pastry on his chin. The entire Wellington—gone. My hours of work, my mother’s final test, vanished into his greedy maw.

“Hey, Leo,” he smirked, wiping his mouth with the back of his hand. “Left your little project on the counter. Needed some protein. It was alright. A bit dry, honestly.”

Brenda tittered from the doorway. Dad just looked uncomfortable. “Boys, please. It’s just food. We’ll order pizzas for the dinner.”

Just food. I felt a cold calm settle over me, a stillness I’d learned from watching my mother face her end with dignity. I didn’t scream. I didn’t cry. I simply looked at Chad’s smug face and said, “I hope you enjoyed it.” Because I knew something they didn’t.

A few months prior, after a slew of stolen Amazon packages, I’d installed a tiny, high-definition nanny cam in a fake smoke detector in the kitchen. Cheap, effective. For package thieves. Or, as it turned out, for stepbrothers committing multi-million dollar felonies.

We sat through the will reading in the formal living room. The lawyer, Mr. Thorne, was all sharp angles and solemnity. There were small bequests to charities, the house left jointly to Dad and “my surviving spouse,” which now meant Brenda. Chad got a token $10,000. Then Mr. Thorne got to the final, major clause.

“To my beloved son, Leo,” he read, his voice echoing in the tense room, “I leave the entirety of the Calloway family estate—including the primary residence upon my husband’s passing, the investment portfolios, the vacation property in Maine, and all remaining assets—provided he can faithfully recreate my mother’s signature Beef Wellington for a family gathering. The recipe is contained within the book bequeathed solely to him. This is not merely a culinary challenge, but a test of dedication, of honoring tradition, and of possessing the discernment to protect what is sacred.”

Silence. Then Chad snorted. “So cook it, then. What’s the big deal?”

Mr. Thorne peered over his glasses. “The clause specifies the recreation must be presented and verified. Consumption by the family is the verification.”

Dad paled. Brenda’s eyes darted to Chad, then to me, a slow horror dawning.

I spoke quietly. “I did recreate it. Perfectly. It was resting on the kitchen counter, ready for this dinner.” I turned to Chad. “He ate it. All of it. Just before we came in here.”

Chaos erupted. Chad blustered, calling me a liar. Brenda shrieked that it was a trick. Dad pleaded for calm. Through it all, I remained still.

“I have proof,” I said, my voice cutting through the noise. I pulled out my phone, opened the security app, and handed it to Mr. Thorne. There, in crystal-clear HD, was the time-lapse: me, meticulously preparing the Wellington, sliding it into the oven, pulling out the flawless golden parcel, placing it on the counter, and leaving. Then, minutes later, Chad entering, looking around, and gleefully devouring the entire thing with his hands, taunting the empty air before swaggering out.

The blood drained from Chad’s face. Brenda looked like she might vomit. Dad just stared at the floor.

Mr. Thorne studied the footage, then nodded slowly. “The will stipulates the creation and presentation. The video clearly demonstrates Mr. Leo’s successful creation. The subsequent… destruction… of the dish by another party prior to presentation does not invalidate his fulfillment of the clause. The intent and the capability are documented.”

It took six months of legal wrangling, but it was ironclad. The house, the investments, everything that was truly my mother’s, flowed to me. Dad and Brenda could stay in the house for now, but it was mine. Chad’s $10,000 was quickly swallowed by the credit card debt he’d been accruing, expecting a windfall.

The fallout was nuclear. Brenda moved out, taking Chad to a condo she could barely afford. Dad and I are in a state of fragile, quiet truce, shrouded in his shame and my unspoken grief. The house is too big, too quiet, filled with the ghosts of my mother and the lingering stench of betrayal.

But here’s the final twist, the secret I’ve carried like a sweet, private stone in my heart. The kicker that makes me sometimes, in my darkest moments, smile.

I’ve been a vegetarian since I was fifteen. The cause was a documentary; the commitment, my own. My mother, bless her, always supported it, crafting separate, equally delicious veggie feasts for me.

Her recipe on page forty-seven? It was for Beef Wellington. But in the margin, in her handwriting, was a note: “For my Leo: Substitute the tenderloin with the same weight of pressed, marinated king oyster mushrooms and lentil-walnut pâté. Sear well. It fools everyone.”

That’s what I used. That’s what I mastered. A $30 slab of premium meat substitute, mushrooms, and lentils, transformed through my mother’s wisdom and my hands into a thing of such convincing beauty and aroma that a carnivore like Chad was utterly fooled.

He didn’t just steal my inheritance. He stole a vegetarian’s lovingly crafted, plant-based masterpiece. He gambled millions, and lost, on a mouthful of mushrooms.

So, AITA? I didn’t force-feed him. I didn’t lure him. I simply knew his nature, protected what was mine with a camera my mother’s money bought, and let his own greed be his downfall. I fulfilled my mother’s last wish to the letter, and in doing so, protected the legacy she wanted me to have.

The ghost of beef Wellington in our house has finally been laid to rest. And for the first time since she died, I think my mom would be proud.
