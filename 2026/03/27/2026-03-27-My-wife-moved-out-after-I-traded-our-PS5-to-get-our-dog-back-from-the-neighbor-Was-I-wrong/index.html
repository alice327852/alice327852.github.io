My wife moved out after I traded our PS5 to get our dog back from the neighbor. Was I wrong?

Posted on 2026-03-27 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Title: TIFU by trading my PS5 for my dog and lost my marriage in the process.

Subreddit: r/TrueOffMyChest

User: ChillGamerDude

Hey Reddit. I don’t even know where to start. My life just got atomized, and it’s my fault. I’m sitting on my apartment floor with my back against a half-empty IKEA bookshelf, my dog Max asleep with his head on my lap, and a stack of papers next to me that say “Dissolution of Marriage.” The silence is so loud it’s ringing in my ears. All because of a PlayStation 5 and the worst neighbor imaginable.

Some background. I’m a simple guy. I work in IT, I love my wife Sarah (well, ex-wife soon, I guess), I adore my dog Max—a goofy, golden retriever mix with one floppy ear—and my main hobby is gaming. After a long day of dealing with server outages, there’s nothing better than sinking into the couch with Max curled up at my feet, controller in hand, and just… checking out for a bit. Sarah would sometimes roll her eyes, but she got it. It was my “guy time.” We’d been saving for a new home security system, and the money I got from my bonus last year, which I’d used to finally snag a PS5 after months of trying, was technically part of that fund. A “temporary diversion,” she’d called it. A argument we’d smoothed over.

Then there’s Karen. Not her real name, but on the Nextdoor app, she’s “KarenTheChooser.” You know the type. Posts about “noisy children” at 3 PM, demands for free lawn care from teenagers “to build their character,” and a constant stream of sob stories about her precious, angelic son, Brayden, who apparently needed the latest everything for his “anxiety.” We’d successfully avoided her for two years, until three weeks ago.

Max disappeared.

I let him out into our fenced backyard one evening while I started making dinner. Five minutes later, I called him. Nothing. The side gate, which I know I latched, was swinging open. My heart just dropped. Panic is a cold, metallic taste in your mouth. Sarah was out with friends. I spent the night driving around, calling his name until I was hoarse, posting on every lost pet forum, sobbing in my car.

The next morning, bleary-eyed and desperate, I saw it on Nextdoor. From KarenTheChooser.

“Found a scared little doggie wandering near the park! So sweet, just trembling. He’s safe with me for now, but oh my, I’m so broke this month I can hardly afford food for poor little Brayden, let alone this sweet boy! 🥺 Any suggestions?”

The photo. It was Max. Curled up on a stained beige carpet, looking confused. My Max.

I exploded with relief. I immediately commented, DMed her, called her. “That’s my dog! That’s Max! Thank you so much! I’ll come right now!”

Her response was slow, dripping with faux-concern. “Oh, he’s yours? He’s such a darling. He and Brayden have really bonded. It’s been so therapeutic for Brayden’s issues.”

I offered a reward. Cash. $200. She demurred. “It’s not about the money. It’s about doing the right thing for this precious soul.”

The next day, after more agonizing messages where she hinted at how “attached” Brayden was, she laid it out. She called me, her voice a saccharine whisper.

“You know, ChillGamerDude,” (yes, she used my handle), “Brayden has been through so much. His therapist says interactive gaming can be a crucial emotional outlet. He’s been dreaming of a PS5. It’s the one thing that would… soften the blow of saying goodbye to this sweet baby. It would make letting him go okay. It’s not about value, it’s about my son’s heart.”

I was stunned. A PS5? For my own dog? The manipulation was so blatant it was almost impressive. But I wasn’t thinking. I was in a vortex of grief and relief. All I could see was Max’s confused face in that photo. I imagined him scared, wondering why I wasn’t there. Sarah was away on a work trip. I made the decision alone.

“Okay,” I heard myself say, the word tasting like ash. “Bring Max to the park. I’ll bring the PS5.”

The trade was surreal. There she was, in oversized sunglasses, holding Max’s leash. Her son, a sullen teenager glued to his phone, didn’t even look up. Max saw me and went berserk, whining, pulling, his whole body wagging. I handed over the console, box, controllers, the whole bundle. She didn’t even say thank you. Just a smug, “A mother does what she must,” before shuffling away.

Max covered my face in slobbery kisses. I cried. I had him back. That was all that mattered.

For two days, it was bliss. Max was glued to my side. Sarah came home, was overjoyed about Max, and when I told her how I got him back, her face clouded over.

“You gave her your PS5? The one from the security fund?”
“It was for Max,” I said, defensive.
“It was a scam, David. A transparent one. You let her play you.”
We argued. It was tense, but it faded. Or so I thought.

Then, I saw Brayden’s TikTok. A mutual friend’s kid showed me. There he was, in what was clearly Karen’s living room, holding up my DualSense controller. The caption: “Shoutout to my mom for being the GOAT and finally copping me the PS5! She said she got a hell of a deal on it too! #Blessed #GamerLife”

The rage was instantaneous, pure, and clarifying. A “deal.” She sold it. This wasn’t about therapy. It was a grift. But something deeper nagged at me. The gate. Max never, ever escaped. He didn’t know how to lift the latch.

I went full detective mode. I walked the fence line again. And there, on the inside of the gatepost, faint but visible in the right light, was a smudge of hot pink nail polish. Karen always had garish, peeling pink nails. I remembered her complaining on Nextdoor about the “vandal” who’d chipped her polish on her mailbox.

The pieces clicked with horrible certainty. She’d seen Max. Known he was my world. Seen me playing fetch with him from her upstairs window, which overlooked our yard. She’d seen the PS5 box in my recycling weeks ago. It was all premeditated.

I called the non-emergency police line. I presented my evidence: the nail polish, the timeline, the Nextdoor posts, the TikTok video proving it wasn’t about therapy but resale. The officers were surprisingly serious. They went to her house.

The confrontation was everything. She answered the door, all innocence, until she saw me with the cops. She broke down fast. Admitted she’d “borrowed” the dog to teach me a lesson about “community sharing” because I’d never loaned her my “expensive gaming system” when Brayden asked. She’d unlatched the gate, whistled for Max (who was friendly to a fault), and led him away. The “therapy” story was her masterpiece of guilt-tripping.

They arrested her for theft and fraud. The PS5 was long gone, sold on Facebook Marketplace for cash. I felt a savage jolt of victory. Justice. I drove home, my heart pounding, ready to finally close this awful chapter with Sarah.

I walked in, ready to proclaim our victory. “We got her, babe. She confessed. It’s over.”

Sarah was sitting at the kitchen table, not with a celebratory glass of wine, but with a stern, exhausted expression. Her laptop was open.

“David,” she said. Her voice was flat. Cold. “The police called me. To verify the story.”

“And? We won!”
“We? You won. You got your revenge. But let’s look at what you did. You traded a $500 console, money earmarked for our home’s security, because you got emotional. You didn’t call me. You didn’t think. You were manipulated by the most obvious con artist on the planet.”

My triumph curdled in my stomach. “It was for Max! You love him too!”
“Of course I love him! But this isn’t about Max. This is about judgment. This is about partnership. That money was for cameras, for sensors, to keep us both safe. You unilaterally decided it was a fair trade for a problem she created. You were so focused on the villain next door, you didn’t see the breach in your own walls.”

“So I’m the villain now?” I shot back, hurt.
“No. You’re the fool. And I can’t build a life with a fool. I can’t trust you to make rational decisions when it counts. What happens when we have a kid, and there’s a crisis? Do you trade the car for a magic bean?”

The fight was nuclear. Every insecurity, every past financial hiccup, was weaponized. My gaming, my “naivete,” my “emotional childishness.” The trust was incinerated. She stayed with a friend that night.

A week of silence followed. A week of me trying to apologize, to explain, to fix it. Max sensed the tension, resting his head on my knee whenever I sat down, staring at me with his big, understanding eyes.

Then, today, I came home from work. The key felt weird in the lock. I pushed the door open.

The living room was… different. Half-empty. Her plants were gone from the windowsill. The decorative pillows she loved. The TV console—the spot where my PS5 once lived—was just a blank, dark space.

And there, on the kitchen counter, next to my now-solo coffee mug, was a thick envelope. From a lawyer.

Next to it, neatly stacked, were boxes containing my things. My books, my gaming guides (for a console I no longer owned), my old collectibles.

She’d packed for me. While I was at work. The ultimate, final admin.

I didn’t even get a face-to-face goodbye. Just papers, and a void where my life used to be.

So now I’m here. In this apartment we’re both still on the lease for, but where only I remain. Max is my sole comfort. The police say there might be restitution, but it’s slow. Karen’s life is unraveling too, facing charges, but that’s cold comfort.

The revenge I thought was so satisfying was just the detonator. Karen broke into my yard, but Sarah just walked out the front door. And I was the one who, in my desperate love for my dog, handed them both the tools to do it.

I look at the empty space below the TV. No glowing blue light. No whir of a fan. Just dust, and the phantom memory of a life that felt whole. Max whines softly and licks my hand. He’s all I have left. And as I sit here in the quiet, I realize the game is over, and I never even got to press start.
