My sister-in-law destroyed my $5,000 PC. My calm response changed everything.

Posted on 2026-03-20 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

r/ProRevenge

Title: Choosing Beggar Ex-Sister-in-Law threw a tantrum and destroyed my $5K gaming rig. Found out I had a secret savings account for “gaming emergencies.” Let’s just say I complied with her demands for compensation.

For a decade, I’ve been a proud, dedicated member of the “Dawnbreakers,” a guild that’s seen me through more digital wars and boss fights than I can count. My sanctuary was my home office, dominated by “The Obelisk,” a top-tier gaming PC that was my pride and joy. It’s also what made me Target #1 for my now ex-sister-in-law, Chloe.

Chloe married my brother, Mark, eight years ago. She was a textbook Choosing Beggar from day one. Her two kids from a previous relationship were her primary weapons. Every family gathering was a shakedown. “The boys need a console for hand-eye coordination!” “That old laptop you gave them is so slow, it’s hurting their grades!” I’d donate my previous-gen gear, only to get texts days later complaining about “scratches” or that it “wasn’t the current model.” Mark, bless his heart, was blind to it. He’d just shrug and say, “That’s just Chloe being passionate about the kids.”

The inciting incident was a Tuesday. I was between work meetings, idly browsing a subreddit for a game we were hyped for. My doorbell rang, then immediately turned into pounding. It was Chloe. No call, no text. She stormed past me the second I opened the door, her two sons trailing behind like disinterested ducklings.

“We need to talk about the boys’ future,” she declared, planting herself in front of The Obelisk. Its RGB lights pulsed softly, like a wary heartbeat.

“Chloe, I’m kind of in the middle of—”

“They’re falling behind in school. All their projects require a powerful computer. You have this… this monster just sitting here, for games.” She spat the word like it was vulgar. “It’s selfish. You need to donate this to them. Today.”

I was stunned. “Donate? Chloe, this is my work and my hobby. It’s worth over five thousand dollars.”

“Exactly! Imagine what it could do for their education! You can just build another one. Mark says you’re always tinkering.”

I took a deep breath, trying to channel my guild leader’s diplomacy during a wipe. “No. I’m not giving you my PC. I’d be happy to help you find some good deals on components, or look into educational grants—”

Her face contorted. It was a mask of pure, entitled rage I’d never seen before. “You’re a selfish uncle. A selfish person. You’d deny your own flesh and blood—“

“They’re not my flesh and blood, Chloe, and this isn’t a discussion.” I pointed to the door. “Please leave.”

What happened next felt like a slow-motion nightmare. On my desk, next to my mousepad, was a 2-liter bottle of soda I’d opened during my last gaming session. Chloe’s eyes darted to it. Without another word, she snatched it up, unscrewed the cap, and before I could even process the movement, she upended the entire bottle directly into the top vents of The Obelisk’s tower.

The sound. Oh god, the sound. A horrific, fizzy, crackling gurgle. The RGB lights flickered, stuttered, and died with a faint pop. A brown, sticky waterfall poured out of the seams and pooled on my hardwood floor. The smell of sugar and ozone filled the air.

I was frozen. Her boys’ eyes were wide. Chloe slammed the empty bottle on my desk. “There. Now you have to get a new one. Maybe you’ll think of the boys next time.”

She turned on her heel and marched out, her sons scrambling after her. I just stood there, staring at the corpse of my sanctuary. The grief was immediate and physical. Five years of saving, researching, building. Thousands of hours of saved games, mod configurations, personal projects… gone. The sheer, wanton malice of it took my breath away.

Mark came over that night, shamefaced. He’d finally gotten the full story from his terrified kids. He helped me assess the damage. It was total. GPU, motherboard, CPU, power supply—everything was a sticky, corroded mess. He was devastated, for me and for his crumbling marriage. “I’m so sorry,” he kept repeating. “I’ll pay for it. I don’t know how, but I will.”

I shook my head. “This isn’t on you, Mark.” And then, looking at the carcass of my PC, I remembered something. A secret I’d kept for ten years.

Back in my early Dawnbreaker days, we had a running joke: “Gaming Emergency Fund.” When a new, ludicrously expensive GPU launched and we resisted the urge, we’d say, “I’m putting that $800 in the Guild Fund.” I took it literally. I opened a high-yield savings account, invisible to my regular finances. Every time I wanted a premium skin but didn’t buy it? $20 in the fund. Decided to keep my current monitor for another year? $500 in the fund. Resisted the hype for the latest, greatest CPU? $350 in the fund. It wasn’t money I was “saving”; it was money I was actively choosing not to spend on incremental upgrades. A decade of forgone indulgence. I’d log in sometimes, just to watch the number grow, a private joke between me and my future self.

I logged in that night. The balance: $28,417.62.

A cold, calm resolve settled over me. The grief was still there, but now it had a purpose.

The next week, I got a letter from a lawyer. Chloe was suing me. I had to read it three times. The claims were unhinged: I had caused her children “severe emotional distress” by creating an environment of “technological haves and have-nots.” My refusal to donate The Obelisk constituted “intentional infliction of emotional distress” on her. She sought compensation for their therapy, for “educational setbacks,” and for her own “pain and suffering.” She was asking for $15,000.

Mark, now fully awakened and filing for divorce, was my rockstar. He had security camera footage from his own doorbell cam, showing Chloe bragging to a friend about “teaching that nerd a lesson by frying his precious toy.” He had text messages where she admitted to the act, saying I “deserved it.” He handed it all to me and my newly hired lawyer.

We went to mediation. Chloe showed up, smug, convinced her performance as a wronged mother would net her a settlement. Her lawyer, a bored-looking man, laid out her “simple” demand: “My client just wants to be made whole. She wants the value of the educational tool you selfishly withheld from those children replaced. That’s all. The exact value.”

The mediator, a weary woman with sharp eyes, looked at me. “Do you understand the claim?”

I nodded. “I do. She wants me to replace the exact value of what I… withheld.”

“Yes,” Chloe hissed. “Just pay what you owe.”

My lawyer slid a document across the table. “My client is prepared to offer a full accounting of the value that was lost.”

Chloe’s lawyer picked it up. His eyebrows slowly climbed his forehead.

Itemized Invoice for Total Loss Incurred:

  • Item 1: Custom Gaming PC (“The Obelisk”) – Specifications & Receipts Attached.

    • Replacement Cost: $5,200.00

  • Item 2: Lost Data & Game Progress.

    • Estimated Total Playtime on Affected Software/Profiles: 5,000 hours.
    • Calculation of Value: Federal Minimum Wage ($7.25/hr) for time invested.
    • Subtotal: $36,250.00

  • Item 3: Plaintiff’s Claim Precedent & Emotional Distress.

    • If the refusal to donate property constitutes infliction of emotional distress, then the malicious destruction of said property constitutes severe, actionable distress to its owner.
    • Quantified Distress (per plaintiff’s own methodology): $6,550.00

  • GRAND TOTAL CLAIM VALUE: $48,000.00

Chloe’s lawyer let out a short, sharp bark of laughter before he could stop himself. Chloe was purple. “This is bullshit! You can’t charge for game time!”

“You’re claiming damages for your children’s unquantified ‘educational setbacks,’” my lawyer said evenly. “We’re claiming damages for very quantifiable destruction of property and time. You established the precedent of assigning monetary value to non-physical harm.”

The mediator studied the footage from Mark. She studied Chloe’s boastful texts. “Mrs. Wilkins,” she said, her voice icy. “You admit to destroying this property?”

“He was hoarding it!” Chloe shouted.

“That’s a ‘yes.’” The mediator turned to me. “Given that you are the clear wronged party here, what would you accept to settle this?”

I leaned forward. “She said she just wanted the ‘exact value’ of what she claims I withheld replaced. I’m willing to comply with that specific demand. Just the physical PC.”

Chloe’s eyes lit up with greedy triumph. She thought she’d won. “Fine. The five thousand.”

“Five thousand, two hundred,” I corrected. “From the invoice.”

“Whatever. Just write the check.”

I pulled my checkbook from my inner pocket. It was from my regular bank account, but the funds I’d transferred in that morning came from a different source entirely. I wrote out the amount with careful precision: Five thousand two hundred and 00/100 dollars. I slid it across to her lawyer.

“The matter is settled,” the mediator said, closing her folder. “Case dismissed.”

Chloe snatched the check from her lawyer, a vicious smile on her face. It was the last time I ever saw her.

The payoff was in the aftermath.

Mark’s divorce was finalized quickly, her behavior in mediation used as evidence of her instability. I, meanwhile, went on a spree. With the remaining $22,800 of my Guild Fund, I commissioned “The Obelisk II: Liquid Retribution.” It was a masterpiece of over-engineering, with custom cooling loops, a GPU more powerful than my old one, and a case that looked like a monument.

Then, I threw a “PC Wake.” I invited the entire Dawnbreaker guild, Mark, and a few close friends. We had a moment of silence for the original Obelisk, played its funeral dirge (the Skyrim theme), and then unveiled the new beast. We livestreamed it. We toasted with expensive energy drinks. We played our favorite game at max settings, the frames silky smooth. I posted pictures everywhere: #PhoenixPC #GuildFund #LiquidRetribution.

A mutual friend told me later that Chloe had to cash my check immediately to pay her now-former lawyer. He’d insisted on upfront payment after the mediation clown show. So she had to take the money, the money that came directly from a fund built on a decade of saying “no” to upgrades—a fund that existed because of people like her, who felt entitled to the fruits of others’ patience.

Every time she sees that check image in her bank records, it reads: MEMO: Replacement Value. Per Your Demand.

And every time someone from our old social circle likes or comments on a picture of The Obelisk II, with its serene, coolant-filled tubes and blinding performance, she’s reminded. She destroyed a $5,200 PC. And in return, she funded a $28,000 legend.

The #GuildFund build isn’t just a computer. It’s a monument to patience, to community, and to the sweet, quiet power of malicious compliance. And I hear its RGB lights glow a little brighter, knowing they live rent-free in her head.
