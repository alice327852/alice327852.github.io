She Shredded My Suit When I Wouldn't Miss My Raid. So I Took Her To Court.

Posted on 2026-03-19 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Subreddit: r/TrueOffMyChest
Title: My (now ex) wife destroyed my $5K gaming rig because I wouldn’t miss my raid for her “final dress fitting.” It got so much worse.

I need to get this out. It’s been a whirlwind, but the dust is finally settling in the best way possible. This is a story of a shattered computer, shredded clothes, and the moment I realized I’d been engaged to a stranger for three years.

My name is Mike, and I’m a gamer. Not just a casual “play some FIFA” guy, but an avid MMO player. For over eight years, I’ve been part of the same guild in Elderon’s Call. We’re “The Forgotten Vanguard,” a tight-knit group of maybe fifteen people spread across the globe. We’ve celebrated births, mourned losses, and helped each other through job losses and breakups. Every Tuesday and Thursday, 8 PM to 11 PM EST, we raid. It’s sacred, non-negotiable time. My fiancée, Sarah, knew this. Or, I thought she did.

Sarah was… high-maintenance. That’s the polite term. Our 18-month engagement was less a journey to marriage and more a military campaign she was orchestrating. Every conversation became about linen samples, floral arrangements, or seating charts. My hobby, my “nights with your internet friends,” was a constant point of friction. She’d call it “childish,” a “waste of time,” especially after I built my new PC. A beauty it was—a self-built, water-cooled monster with a 4090, nicknamed “The Bastion” by my guild. $5K of my own savings, pieced together with care. It was my pride and joy, my portal to my friends.

The incident happened on a Tuesday. Three weeks before the wedding. Sarah texted me at 4 PM: “Final dress fitting! My mom and the bridesmaids are meeting us at the boutique at 7:30. Be ready.”

My blood went cold. 7:30 PM. Raid start was 8 PM. The raid wasn’t just any raid; it was the culmination of a three-month campaign to unlock the “Vault of the Fallen Star.” That night, we were facing the final boss. We’d wiped on it for weeks. This was our scheduled, coordinated final attempt. Everyone had cleared their schedules.

I called her. “Sarah, honey, you know I have my raid tonight. It’s the big one. Can I meet you at the boutique right after work, at 6? I can see the dress then, be home by 7, and you can go back with your mom and the girls for whatever you need at 7:30.”

Silence. Then the ice. “Mike. This is my final fitting. The most important dress of my life. You expect me to just… accommodate your video game?”

“It’s not just a game, Sarah. It’s a commitment I made to fourteen other people. We’ve had this date planned for a month. You never mentioned the fitting.”

“Well, I’m mentioning it now,” she snapped. “The boutique is holding time for us. For the couple. You will be there, from 7:30 to whenever we’re finished. It’s non-negotiable.”

I took a deep breath, trying to be the compromiser. “What if I come for the first hour, give my approval, and then head home? Or I come after the raid? I can be there by 11:15.”

“So you want me to tell my mother, my sisters, and my best friends that my future husband would rather play a computer game than be present for his own wedding preparations? Is that the man I’m marrying?”

The guilt-trip was heavy, but for the first time, it didn’t work. I felt a spark of defiance. “Sarah, I’ve attended every other fitting. I’ve nodded at a hundred different shades of white. I’ve spent tens of thousands on this wedding without a word of complaint. This one night, for three hours, I’m asking for the same respect for my time that I’ve given yours.”

She hung up on me.

I was torn, sick to my stomach. At 6 PM, I texted the guild chat: “Family emergency might make me late. Start without me if you have to.” I felt like a traitor. Then I texted Sarah: “I’ll be at the boutique at 6:45. I’ll stay until 7:30, then I have to leave. I love you, and the dress is beautiful, I know it is. Let’s do this.”

Her reply was instant: “Don’t bother.”

I took that as acceptance. I logged into Discord at 7:55 PM, my heart pounding. “Hey guys, sorry, crisis averted. I’m here.”

A chorus of “Mike!” and “Let’s go!” greeted me. The relief was immense. We loaded in, buffed up, and began the intricate dance of the boss fight. For forty minutes, I was in another world. The focus, the teamwork, the sheer joy of it. At 8:45 PM, I needed to hit the bathroom. “Bio break, 2 mins, don’t wipe without me!” I joked, muting my mic and sprinting down the hall.

I was gone maybe three minutes.

When I opened the door to my office, the smell hit me first. Acrid, sweet. Then the sight.

Sarah was standing over my desk, an empty wine glass in her hand. My PC, “The Bastion,” was humming oddly, a low, gurgling sound. A dark, purple-red waterfall was dripping from the top vents, down the beautiful tempered glass side panel, and pooling on my desk mat, seeping into the cables. The case was open. She’d poured an entire glass of Cabernet Sauvignon directly into the heart of my machine.

On the floor, next to her foot, was my custom mechanical keyboard. The one my guild had gifted me for my birthday. She’d ripped it from the desk and smashed it. Keycaps were scattered like teeth across the carpet.

I just stared. My brain couldn’t process it. The raid chatter was still coming from my headphones, now lying on the desk, speckled with wine. “…Mike, you back? We’re getting to phase two…”

Sarah looked at me, her face a mask of calm, righteous fury. “Now you don’t have to choose,” she said, her voice eerily steady.

I didn’t say a word. I didn’t scream. I didn’t move. A profound, icy silence filled me. I looked at the dying lights of my PC, the spilled wine like blood on my desk, the shattered keyboard. I looked at her, this woman in my home, holding the murder weapon.

I turned around, walked out of the room, out of the house, got in my car, and drove. I drove for two hours, parked by a lake, and just sat there in the dark. The shock was so complete it was anesthetic. I didn’t cry. I just… shut down.

I spent the night at a hotel. I sent one text to my guild officer: “Real family emergency. PC destroyed. Can’t come back. So sorry.” The immediate flood of support, the offers to help, the concern—it was a lifeline. It also highlighted the stark, devastating contrast. My “childish” friends were offering to Venmo me money for a new GPU before even asking what happened.

The next morning, after a sleepless night, I went back to the house. I needed clothes. I needed to assess the damage. I needed to… I didn’t know what.

Sarah was gone. The house was quiet. My office was a crime scene, untouched. The wine had dried into a sticky, purple crust. I took pictures. Dozens of pictures. Close-ups of the components, the serial numbers, the keyboard carnage.

Then I went to our walk-in closet.

And that’s when the true, psychotic scale of it hit me.

Every. Single. Item. Of my clothing was destroyed.

My two bespoke suits for the wedding and my brother’s wedding last year—slashed to ribbons with what I later learned were her fabric shears. My band t-shirts from concerts I’d loved—neatly sliced across the graphics. My jeans, every pair, shredded at the knees and thighs. My favorite, worn-in leather jacket, gouged. My socks, my underwear, my ties—all cut into useless pieces. It was systematic. It was meticulous. It was an annihilation of me. The pile of fabric in the middle of the closet floor looked like the remains of a person.

My knees buckled. I sank to the floor, my hands gripping my hair. The silent fury of the night before evaporated, replaced by a trembling, all-consuming rage and a grief so deep it stole my breath. My grandfather’s vintage tour shirt… gone. The suit I wore when I got my promotion… gone. Everything.

The front door opened. Sarah walked in, carrying a coffee, looking fresh and cheerful. She saw me in the closet doorway, my face pale, my hands shaking.

“Good, you’re home,” she said, as if commenting on the weather. She walked over and peered past me at the devastation she’d wrought. A small, satisfied smile played on her lips.

“Now you have nothing to distract you from me and the wedding,” she said, her tone bright, almost helpful. “We can go shopping for appropriate clothes together. A fresh start.”

That sentence. The sheer, unadulterated madness of it. The absolute conviction that this was not just justified, but corrective. It was like a bucket of ice water. In that moment, any love, any lingering doubt, any fragment of hope I had, died.

I looked at her, this beautiful, smiling stranger, and I spoke very slowly, very calmly. “Get out.”

She blinked. “What?”

“Get out of this house. Now. Or I will call the police and have you removed.”

The smile vanished. “This is my house too! You’re overreacting! It’s just stuff!”

I picked up my phone, my thumb hovering over 9-1-1. “It’s tens of thousands of dollars of my stuff. And you are a vandal. Get. Out.”

She left, spluttering about ungrateful men and cold feet. I didn’t listen. I called my best friend, Chris. He was there in twenty minutes. He saw the office, saw the closet, and without a word, just hugged me. “You’re staying with me,” he said.

That afternoon, with Chris as a witness, I filed a police report. I presented the photos, the receipts I could find for the PC parts and the suits, estimates for the rest. The responding officer’s eyes widened at the closet. “This is… extensive,” he said, writing carefully. It was being classified as felony destruction of property.

Next: a lawyer. A shark of a divorce attorney named Angela. She listened, took notes, and her eyes hardened. “We’ll get an emergency restraining order based on the destructive behavior. We’ll file first. And we’ll serve her.”

A plan crystallized in my numb mind. I knew Sarah’s schedule. I knew her bridal shower was this Saturday, at her mother’s house. All her friends, her family, her perfect, picturesque celebration.

“Can the papers be served there?” I asked Angela.

She smiled a thin, professional smile. “They can be served anywhere she can be legally found.”

The next few days were a blur of legal paperwork, insurance calls, and the profound kindness of my friends. My guild, upon hearing the full story, started a fund. They didn’t just send money; three of them who lived within driving distance showed up at Chris’s place one Saturday with boxes of components. We built a new PC together, on his kitchen table. We called it “Phoenix.” I cried when it booted up.

The day of the bridal shower, I was a knot of nervous energy. I was at Chris’s, refreshing my email. At 2:17 PM, I got a message from my lawyer: “Served.”

Then my phone exploded.

Voicemails. Texts. From Sarah, her mother, her sister. Screaming, crying, pleading, threatening. I read one from Sarah: “HOW DARE YOU HUMILIATE ME LIKE THIS IN FRONT OF EVERYONE! YOU’VE RUINED EVERYTHING! YOU’RE A MONSTER!”

I blocked every number. I muted every notification. I sat back, took a deep breath, and felt a weight lift that I hadn’t even known was crushing me.

The fallout was nuclear. Her family, initially hostile, began to fracture when the police report details and photos (leaked by a “source,” maybe a bridesmaid with a conscience) circulated. The narrative of the “groom with cold feet” shattered against the reality of the wine-soaked computer and the mountain of shredded clothes. Mutual friends quietly reached out to me. “We had no idea,” they said. “She seemed so… perfect.”

The wedding was, obviously, cancelled. My lawyer is currently navigating the dissolution of our shared life. The property destruction case is proceeding. I am told Sarah is contesting everything, claiming “extreme emotional distress” made her do it. It won’t fly.

I’m in therapy. I’m learning that what I experienced was abuse, not just a disagreement. I’m rebuilding, literally and figuratively.

Last night, the Forgotten Vanguard finally downed the Fallen Star. I was there, on “Phoenix,” my new keyboard clacking happily. We cheered on Discord, a cacophony of joy from New York, California, London, and Sydney. After the victory screenshots, one of my guildmates, an older woman named Eleanor who I’ve never met but feel closer to than most relatives, said softly, “We’re proud of you, Mike. For more than just your DPS.”

So, that’s it. My ex-fiancée destroyed a $5K gaming rig because I wouldn’t miss my raid. Then she destroyed my entire wardrobe to try and destroy me.

But my guild helped me build a new PC.

And my lawyer is helping me build a new life.
