My roommate ate my $200 anniversary dinner. I bought a DNA test kit to prove it.

Posted on 2026-03-21 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Title: My roommate ate my $200 anniversary meal. Security footage caught them. They said it wasn’t them. So I got a DNA test.

Subreddit: r/ProRevenge

Body:

I’m a planner. It’s who I am. My wife, Chloe, and I have our 5th anniversary next week, but with her nursing schedule, she’s pulling a 12-hour shift on the actual day. So, I planned a perfect recreation of our first real date for the night before. We’d gone to this tiny, impossibly romantic Italian place that’s long since closed. The meal was herb-crusted rack of lamb with a red wine reduction and truffle risotto. We’ve talked about it for years. I was going to bring it back to life.

Enter my roommate, Mark. “Lazy” doesn’t begin to cover it. He’s been “between opportunities” (his words) for the eight months he’s lived here. He pays his share of the rent, usually, from a mysterious inheritance he vaguely references, but he never, and I mean never, buys groceries. Our agreement was clear: shared staples (milk, bread), personal everything else. Yet, my Greek yogurts would vanish. Slices of my expensive sourdough would disappear. “I just had a bite, dude, relax,” he’d say with a shrug. We’d had The Talk three times. Promises were made. Lies were told.

For the anniversary meal, I spared no expense. The lamb racks from the organic butcher: $85. The black truffle for the risotto: $60. The specific herbs, the good Parmigiano-Reggiano, the bottle of Barolo for the reduction—another $55 easy. I took the afternoon off work. The kitchen was my sanctuary. I rendered shallots, reduced the wine, seared the lamb. The risotto was a labor of love, stirred constantly, the truffle shaved in at the last second. The aroma was unreal. It was 4:30 PM. Chloe would be home at 7:00. I plated our portions perfectly, covered them with cloches, and slid them into the oven (off, but still warm) to keep perfect. The remaining lamb and a good portion of risotto were in their respective pans on the stove, my planned leftovers for a glorious lunch tomorrow.

I ran upstairs to shower and change. I was gone maybe 25 minutes.

I came downstairs, the scent of garlic and rosemary still hanging in the air, and walked into the kitchen. The oven was empty. My beautifully plated meals—gone. The pans on the stove—licked clean. A single plate, slick with grease and grains of risotto, sat in the sink. A fork rested on top.

My heart just… dropped. It wasn’t anger at first. It was a cold, hollow disbelief. This couldn’t be happening. I checked the fridge, the trash, like a crazy person. Nothing. Just that goddamn dirty plate.

I found Mark in the living room, scrolling on his phone, looking profoundly at ease. “Mark. The food in the oven. The pans. Did you…?”

He didn’t even look up. “What food?”

“The lamb. The risotto. I made it for mine and Chloe’s anniversary. It’s gone.”

He finally glanced at me, a picture of innocence. “Wasn’t me. You probably forgot you ate it. Or maybe you didn’t make as much as you thought.”

The audacity was a physical slap. “Mark, I plated it. I put it in the oven to keep warm. It’s gone. There’s a dirty plate in the sink.”

“Not mine,” he said, returning to his phone. “I had a protein shake. Maybe you sleep-cook, bro.”

That’s when the cold disbelief crystallized into pure, white-hot rage. But I didn’t yell. I just stared at him. And then it clicked. The object.

Two weeks ago, after my premium coffee started disappearing, I’d bought a pet cam. Officially for when we eventually get a dog, but let’s be real—it was for the kitchen. It was a tiny, unobtrusive thing on a bookshelf, angled to cover the fridge and stove. It recorded motion-activated clips to the cloud.

I didn’t say a word. I turned, walked to my home office, and logged into the cloud account on my laptop. My hands were shaking.

There it was. A motion alert at 4:41 PM. I clicked the clip.

High-definition, crystal clear. Mark sauntered into the frame. He opened the oven, peered in, and a wide, greedy grin spread across his face. He took out my beautifully plated meals first, setting them on the counter. He lifted the cloche off Chloe’s plate, looked at it, and then… he picked up the rack of lamb with his BARE HANDS and took a huge bite from the end. He chewed, grinning, then did the same to the other plate. He then tossed the ravaged racks back onto the plates, like they were drumsticks at a barbarian feast. He turned to the stove, grabbed the saucepan of risotto, and used the serving spoon to shovel huge mouthfuls directly into his mouth. He did the same with the remaining lamb from the pan, tearing at it with his fingers. For five solid minutes, he gorged. He didn’t even re-plate anything. He ate like a hyena, straight from the pans and our anniversary plates. At one point, he grabbed my bottle of Barolo from the counter and took a long swig straight from the bottle. Then he belched, piled everything into the sink, rinsed one plate and a fork, and sauntered out of frame.

I watched it twice. I saved the video. I downloaded it. I backed it up in three places. Then I walked back to the living room, my laptop in hand.

“Mark. Last chance. Did you eat my food?”

He sighed, put his phone down with theatrical exasperation. “Dude, how many times? No. It. Wasn’t. Me.”

I opened my laptop, turned the screen to him, and hit play.

I watched his face. The initial flicker of shock was immediately buried under a landslide of brazen defiance. He watched the entire clip, his jaw set. When it ended, he didn’t miss a beat.

He snorted. “Oh, that? That’s not me.”

I actually laughed. A sharp, disbelieving bark. “What?”

“That’s my cousin, Jason. He was over earlier. We look almost identical. Same build, same hair. He’s a prankster. He must have done it.”

My blood went from boiling to a strange, eerie calm. The lie was so insane, so insultingly stupid, that it bypassed anger and entered a new territory. This wasn’t just about food anymore. This was a declaration of war.

“Your cousin,” I repeated flatly.
“Yep. Jason.”
“Who you’ve never mentioned in eight months.”
“We’re not close. Lookalike cousins, though. Weird genetics.”
“And he just happened to stop by in the 25-minute window I was upstairs, knew exactly where my hidden anniversary meal was, ate it like a wild animal, and left without you acknowledging him?”
Mark shrugged. “Like I said. Prankster. You should be flattered, honestly. He only does that to people he likes.”

That was it. The Rubicon was crossed.

“Okay,” I said, closing my laptop. “Okay, Mark.”

I walked back to the kitchen. Using a clean plastic bag, I carefully, meticulously picked up the fork from the sink—the fork he had rinsed, but which still had microscopic bits of my food, and more importantly, his saliva, on it. I sealed it in the bag. Then I walked back to the living room, holding it up.

“Since you’re so sure it was your mysterious, never-before-seen cousin Jason,” I said, my voice chillingly pleasant, “I’m sure you won’t mind this. I’m sending this fork to a private DNA testing lab. I’ll swab my own cheek for comparison to rule out my DNA. If, as you claim, it was Jason, then the DNA on this fork won’t match yours. But if it does… well, then we’ll know you’re a lying, food-stealing asshole and a fraud. I’ll expect reimbursement for the meal, the wine, and the lab fee.”

For the first time, a crack appeared in his smug armor. A flicker of uncertainty in his eyes. But he doubled down. “You’re insane. You’re really going to spend, what, hundreds of dollars on a DNA test over some lamb?”

“It wasn’t some lamb,” I said, my voice finally dropping to a low growl. “It was my anniversary. And yes. Every penny.”

I posted the whole saga that night on r/badroommates. The story, the cost breakdown, Mark’s “cousin” lie, my nuclear option. Chloe was devastated but fiercely supportive. She ordered us pizza and said, “Burn him to the ground.” The Reddit post blew up. Thousands of comments. Awards. People demanded updates.

Mark found the post. He started commenting from his own account. “OP is a drama queen. It was a prank by my cousin. He’s wasting his money on a science project. LOL.” His comments were downvoted into oblivion. The backlash was immediate and brutal. People found his other socials and started flooding him with pictures of lamb and questions about “Cousin Jason.”

The two-week wait for the lab results were the longest of my life. The company emailed when the report was ready. My hands trembled as I opened the PDF.

There it was. In cold, clinical, indisputable language.

Sample A (Fork): DNA Profile Obtained.
Sample B (My Buccal Swab): DNA Profile Obtained.
Conclusion: Sample A and Sample B are excluded as being from the same individual. However, a comparison of Sample A to a voluntarily submitted third-party sample (I’d cheek-swabbed Mark’s toothbrush the morning he left for a “job interview”—a story for another day) showed a 99.97% probability of a direct familial match. In layman’s terms, Sample A originated from Subject Mark.

I printed three copies. One for me, one for Mark, one for the fridge.

He was on the couch, playing video games. I didn’t say a word. I just dropped the report in his lap and played the security footage on the TV above him, on a loop.

He read it. His face drained of color. The controller slipped from his hands.

“The lab fee was $200,” I said quietly. “The meal ingredients were $200. The wine was $50. My time, my emotional distress, and the ruining of my wedding anniversary are priceless, but I’ll settle for $450. Cash, Venmo, or Zelle. You have 24 hours before I file in small claims court and serve you and post the full, unedited security footage with the DNA report to TikTok.”

He stared at the paper, at his own name, at the percentage that sealed his fate. The social media backlash had been a nuisance. This was a concrete, scientific monument to his own shamelessness. He had no lie left. The “cousin” defense was atomized by a double helix.

He didn’t say a word. He transferred the $450 an hour later. The thud of moving boxes started the next morning. He was gone within 48 hours, vanishing in the night like the petty ghost he was.

The community he mocked rallied against him. His online presence is a graveyard. I never did get an apology. I don’t need one.

Chloe and I had our anniversary dinner last weekend. I made the meal again. It was perfect. We ate it straight from the pan, laughing until we cried.

And on a shelf in my office, next to a picture of our wedding, I keep a small shadow box. Inside, mounted on velvet, is a single, ordinary fork. The label beneath it reads: “Exhibit A.” It’s my trophy. A $200 fork. The most satisfying thing I’ve ever owned.
