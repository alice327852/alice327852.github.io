My Boss Demanded My Grandfather’s Watch, So I Gave Her Exactly What She Deserved

Posted on 2026-03-15 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Subreddit: r/AmItheAsshole
Title: AITA for giving my controlling boss a fake watch and letting her dig her own grave?

Body:

I need to know if I’ve crossed a line from justified payback into pure, unadulterated asshole territory. Grab a drink. This is a long one.

For context, I’m 26, and for the last two years, I’ve been a junior marketing associate at a mid-sized, painfully “corporate vibes” firm. My boss, Diana, is the human embodiment of a micromanaging spreadsheet. She didn’t just oversee work; she presided over it like a feudal lord. Every email was cc’d, every Slack message was dissected for tone, and “thinking outside the box” was a punishable offense unless the box was one she herself had constructed.

Then, three months ago, my grandfather died. He was my last living grandparent, the one who taught me how to ride a bike, fix a leaky faucet, and appreciate the quiet dignity of a well-made thing. I was devastated. I took the bereavement leave—three days, which felt obscenely short for a man who shaped my entire world.

Diana’s “sympathy” was a masterpiece of corporate emptiness. A single, curt email: “Sorry for your loss. See attached for the Q3 projections you’ll need to review upon your return.” No card, no flowers, just a reminder that my grief was an inconvenient blip on the quarterly roadmap.

A week after I returned, still moving through fog, a package arrived at the office for me. It was from my grandfather’s lawyer. Inside, nestled in faded velvet, was his watch. A vintage Omega Seamaster, from the 1960s. It wasn’t just a watch; it was the watch. The one he wore every day of his adult life, the one with the scratch on the crystal from the time he saved me from falling off a rockslide. Its value was emotional, incalculable. I held it in my palm, the cool metal a connection to him, and I cried quietly at my desk.

That’s when Diana’s shadow fell over me.

“What’s that?” she asked, her voice slicing through my moment. I, stupidly, showed her. I was vulnerable, and for a second, I forgot who I was talking to.

Her eyes, usually sharp with critique, glazed over with a different kind of hunger. She took it from my hand before I could react. “My God. This is a 1962 Seamaster. Ref. 165.002. The patina on this dial… this is a collector’s item.”

I was stunned she knew. “It was my grandfather’s,” I said quietly, retrieving it from her grip.

She didn’t hear me. The gears were turning. That afternoon, she called me into her glass-walled office.

“Look,” she began, with the tone of someone bestowing wisdom. “I’ve been thinking. I’ve been a tremendous mentor to you, haven’t I? Taken you under my wing, really shaped you. That kind of investment… it’s rare.”

I nodded, wary. Praise from Diana was always a prelude to a demand.

“That watch,” she continued, leaning forward. “On you, it’s a trinket. A sad memento. On me, in this industry, it’s a statement. It speaks of legacy, taste, success. Clients notice these things. Giving it to me would be a… strategic career move. A sign of your gratitude and understanding of how this world works. It could fast-track your advancement here. Think of it as an investment in your future with this company.”

I felt a cold nausea. “Diana, I can’t. It’s my grandfather’s. It’s not for sale or gifting. It’s priceless.”

Her smile tightened into a grimace. “Priceless is a subjective term. And so is ‘job security.’”

Act 2: The Squeeze.

The retaliation was swift and surgical. My projects were reassigned to sycophants. My emails went unanswered for days, only to be replied to with public, nitpicking critiques in all-team threads. I was given a “special project”: manually transcribing five years of archived meeting notes—a task designed to be both demeaning and isolating. My mid-year review, she warned me with a icy smile, was “not looking promising.”

The pressure was a physical weight. Rent was due. Student loans don’t care about your grief or your heirlooms. One Friday, as I was leaving, she cornered me by the printer.

“I had a collector call me,” she said casually. “Saw a picture I’d posted from that charity gala—you remember, the one you weren’t invited to? He estimated the watch at over $25,000. Quite a little nest egg your grandpa left you.” She paused, letting the number hang in the sterile office air. “It’s a shame to see such an asset… underutilized. On the wrong wrist. Almost a waste, really. A waste I’m not sure I can afford to carry on my team.”

The extortion was no longer subtext. It was the text. The watch, or my job.

That weekend, in a haze of despair, I finally opened the other, thicker letter from my grandfather’s lawyer I’d been avoiding. It wasn’t just about the watch. It was about the estate. My grandfather, it turned out, was the discreetly wealthy black sheep of his family. He’d built a quiet empire in industrial supply, a “boring” business that printed money. The lawyer explained that the probate process, slow and complex, was now concluding. I was named the sole heir. Not just to the watch. To everything. The holdings, the company, the investments. The number of zeroes attached to “net worth” made my screen blur.

A furious, crystalline calm settled over me. Diana wanted to play games of power and leverage? Fine.

Act 3: The Play.

I spent a week researching. I found a forum dedicated to vintage watch replicas. I wasn’t looking for a cheap knock-off; I needed a “super replica,” one that would pass the eye test of an arrogant, knowledge-thin braggart like Diana. I found one. It cost me $800. To the inheritance, it was a rounding error. To the plan, it was the perfect prop.

The next Monday, I requested a brief one-on-one. I looked tired, defeated. I’d practiced that look in the mirror.

“Diana,” I said, my voice thick with manufactured resignation. “You were right. About the investment. About my future here.” I pulled out the replica, nestled in a convincing box I’d bought online. “I want you to have it. As a token of my… appreciation.”

Her triumph was naked and glorious. She didn’t even examine it closely. She snapped the box shut, her fingers possessive. “A wise decision. Truly. You won’t regret this. I’ll make sure the review reflects your… improved judgement.”

She began wearing the watch immediately. She’d find reasons to gesture broadly in meetings, letting the light catch the fake crystal. “This old thing?” she’d laugh to clients. “A gift from a protégé. So grateful for my mentorship. It’s about recognizing value, you know?” She’d tell the story of the “priceless heirloom” she’d “inspired” me to part with, a parable of her own influence.

Meanwhile, I met with lawyers. I signed mountains of paper. The holding company that now bore my name had a diverse portfolio. And, as a matter of complete coincidence, it held a significant, non-controlling stake in the firm that employed me—an asset picked up years ago in a broader acquisition. It was enough to get me a seat at a very quiet table.

I said nothing. I did my demeaning tasks. I took her condescension. I let her dig.

Act 4: The Unraveling.

Diana landed a meeting with a major potential client, a luxury goods distributor. She planned a power lunch at the fanciest restaurant in town. The day before, she strutted to my desk. “I’m bringing you along. To observe. To see how it’s done. And,” she added, tapping the fake watch on her wrist, “to see your investment in action.”

The lunch was a spectacle of her self-aggrandizement. The client, a man named Robert with a quiet, assessing gaze, was polite. Diana, halfway through her second martini, finally played her card.

“You know, Robert, in this business, authenticity is everything. Take this watch.” She slid it off her wrist, passing it across the white linen. “A 1962 Omega Seamaster. A family heirloom, gifted to me by a team member I mentored. It’s not just a timepiece; it’s a symbol of legacy and trust.”

Robert took it. He held it. He turned it over. He didn’t say anything for a full minute, his expression unreadable. Diana beamed.

Finally, he cleared his throat. “It’s a very compelling piece, Diana.”

“Isn’t it? The patina alone—”

“The patina,” Robert interrupted gently, almost apologetically, “is chemically induced. Quite well done, actually. The lume on the hands is the wrong shade for the period. And the movement…” He flipped it back to her with a soft, definitive click. “I’m afraid it’s a replica. A very good one, but a replica nonetheless. Probably cost eight hundred, a thousand dollars.”

The silence was atomic. The color drained from Diana’s face, then flooded back in a violent, humiliated crimson. She stared at the watch as if it had bitten her. All her posturing, her stories, her perceived power—exposed as a fraud built on a fraud. Her eyes snapped to me, burning with dawning, horrific understanding.

“You,” she seethed, her voice trembling with rage. She stood up, knocking her chair back. “You little shit. You set me up. Do you have any idea what you’ve done? YOU’RE FIRED! Get out! Clean out your desk, you worthless, ungrateful—”

I remained seated. The other diners were staring. Robert looked profoundly uncomfortable.

I reached into the inner pocket of my jacket, which was newer and finer than anything Diana had ever seen me wear. I pulled out a simple, cream-colored business card. I slid it across the table, past the untouched desserts, until it came to rest in front of her.

She glanced down, ready to sweep it away, and froze.

It bore the logo of our parent holding company. And beneath it, my name. With a single, unambiguous title: Principal Owner & Chair of the Board.

I looked up at her, my voice calm and clear in the hushed restaurant. “Actually, Diana, I own a controlling interest in the holding company that holds the majority stake in our firm. I inherited it. So, you can’t fire me.” I paused, letting the words sink into her crumbling world. “But I can fire you. Effective immediately. For attempted coercion, creating a hostile work environment, and, frankly, for having embarrassingly bad taste in watches. HR and security will meet you at the office to escort you out. Enjoy the rest of your lunch.”

I stood up, nodded politely to a stunned Robert. “Mr. Dawson, my apologies for the scene. My assistant will set up a proper meeting next week. I believe our companies can do great business together. Without the theatrics.”

I walked out, leaving Diana standing there, clutching a fake watch and a reality that had just violently ejected her.

So, Reddit, AITA? The watch was a fake, but the humiliation was real. I could have just quit when I got the inheritance. I could have reported her to HR. Instead, I led her into a trap of her own making and let her publicly destroy herself. It was emotionally satisfying in a way I can’t describe. But part of me wonders if I became a monster to defeat one.
