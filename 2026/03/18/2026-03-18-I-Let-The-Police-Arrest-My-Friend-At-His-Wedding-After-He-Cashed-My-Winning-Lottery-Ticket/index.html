I Let The Police Arrest My Friend At His Wedding After He Cashed My Winning Lottery Ticket

Posted on 2026-03-18 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Subreddit: r/AmITheAsshole (Update)
Title: UPDATE: AITA for calling the cops on my roommate… at his wedding?

Original Post Recap: I (28M) lived with Mark (29M) for three hellish years. He was the stereotype of a bad roommate: perpetually “between jobs,” left dishes to fossilize, and his contribution to rent was a monthly performance of tragic excuses. I was the sucker who kept covering for him, fearing I’d be stuck with the full lease. Then, my grandmother passed. She wasn’t wealthy, but she left me $50,000, with a note saying, “Use this to build a happier life.” I told Mark, trying to share my bittersweet joy. I mentioned offhandedly that I might blow a hundred bucks on scratch-offs for fun, a little superstition she and I had.

One ticket, a $20 “Lucky Diamond” one, won $5,000. I was ecstatic. I taped it to the fridge, a dumb, triumphant ritual. A “good luck charm,” I told Mark. “I’ll cash it tomorrow.” The next day, it was gone. Mark swore he hadn’t seen it, suggested it fell behind the fridge, accused me of misplacing it. I tore the apartment apart. Nothing.

Then, while taking out his trash (my chore, naturally), I saw a crumpled receipt from “Regal Formalwear.” A luxury tuxedo rental. Paid in cash. In his name. Dated the day after my ticket vanished. My blood went cold. I did some social media digging. His fiancée, Chloe, was posting excitedly about their budget wedding—DIY centerpieces, a cash bar. No mention of a sudden $5,000 windfall.

I called the state lottery commission. After verifying my identity and the ticket number (I’d taken a photo, thankfully), they confirmed: it had been cashed two days prior at a licensed claim center downtown. Grand larceny. A felony.

I went to the police with the photo, the receipt, the lottery’s confirmation, and a timeline. The detective, a weary-looking man named Harris, saw the case for what it was. “We can pick him up now,” he said. But I had a question. A terrible, dramatic, possibly assholish question. “His wedding is in two days. What if… we do it there?” Detective Harris raised an eyebrow. “Controlled confrontation. He’ll be surrounded by people, less likely to flee. We can have officers on site. But you’d need to be the one to initiate, to point him out formally in front of us.” A part of me, the part that had cleaned his moldy yogurt cups for the last time, ignited. “I’ll do it.”

So, the original AITA was posted the night before the wedding. Judgement was overwhelmingly NTA, with comments ranging from “This is nuclear and I am here for it” to “Just arrest him now, don’t ruin the bride’s day.” I struggled with that. Chloe seemed sweet, oblivious. But if I acted before, Mark would spin a web of lies. This way, the truth would have its ultimate stage.

The Update:

I bought a new suit. Not a rental. Something with cash from the inheritance, something that felt like armor. The wedding was at a bland, beige banquet hall attached to a mid-tier hotel. I sat in the back, on the groom’s side, a lone island. I saw Mark’s family, people who’d heard stories of his “mooching roommate” who was “always on his case.” They gave me polite, confused nods.

Chloe came down the aisle. She looked genuinely happy, radiant in a simple lace dress. For a moment, my resolve wavered. This would detonate her life, too. But then I saw Mark at the altar, beaming in his obscenely expensive tuxedo, the one my stolen luck had bought. His hair was perfectly styled, his shoes gleaming. He looked like the successful man he’d always pretended to be. The man my stolen money had dressed him as. Any pity evaporated.

The ceremony proceeded. Generic vows about trust and sharing. Each word felt like a screw turning. My heart hammered against my ribs. Detective Harris and another plainclothes officer were posing as venue staff near the back doors. I caught his slight nod.

Then, the moment. The officiant, a jovial man with a microphone, said the words I’d been waiting for. “If anyone here present knows of any reason why this man and this woman should not be joined in holy matrimony, speak now, or forever hold your peace.”

A beat of silence. A roomful of people holding their breath for tradition’s sake, not expecting a thing.

I stood up.

The wooden chair leg scraped loudly on the parquet floor. Two hundred heads swiveled. Chloe’s smile froze. Mark’s eyes found me in the back. Confusion, then dawning horror wiped the smugness from his face. He knew.

“I have a reason,” I said, my voice surprisingly steady, carrying in the hushed room. I didn’t shout. I stated. “Mark is a thief. He stole a winning lottery ticket from me, worth five thousand dollars, and used it to pay for the tuxedo he’s wearing right now.”

A collective gasp ripped through the room. Chloe’s hand flew to her mouth. “What?” she whispered, looking between Mark and me.

“That’s a lie!” Mark barked, his face flushing crimson. “He’s jealous! He’s always been jealous of me and Chloe!”

“I filed a police report three days ago,” I continued, addressing the crowd now, my gaze finally landing on Chloe. “The lottery commission confirmed he cashed my ticket. The receipt for this tuxedo rental, paid in cash, is dated the day after it went missing.” I pointed a finger at him. “You stole from me, Mark. You stole from me for years, but this time you stole a felony.”

That’s when I nodded to the back. The two “venue staff” moved with purpose down the aisle. Detective Harris flashed his badge. The other officer moved to flank Mark.

“Mark [Last Name],” Harris said, his voice official and grave in the wedding-quiet hall, “you are under arrest for grand larceny. You have the right to remain silent…”

Pandemonium. Mark’s mother shrieked. Chloe stumbled back from the altar as if burned. Mark started babbling, “It was a loan! He said I could! Chloe, baby, it’s a misunderstanding!”

But the cuffs came out. The metallic click was the loudest sound I’ve ever heard. They led him away, the tails of his fine tuxedo jacket flapping, his polished shoes shuffling. He kept yelling over his shoulder, a stream of denials and curses aimed at me, until the doors swung shut behind them.

The silence they left was deafening, then shattered by the hysterical sobs of his mother and the furious, confused shouts of the guests. I stood my ground, watching the fallout.

Then Chloe walked toward me. Her veil was askew, her face pale. She stopped a few feet away. “Is it true?” she asked, her voice trembling but clear. “All of it?”

“Yes,” I said, pulling out my phone. I showed her the photo of the ticket on my fridge, the police report case number, the screenshot of my call log with the lottery commission. “He’s been lying to you. To everyone. He’s been unemployed for months. I’ve been covering his share. This… this was just the last straw.”

She stared at the evidence, her eyes filling with tears not of sadness, but of a furious, burning betrayal. She looked at the empty space where her groom had been, at the gawking crowd, then back at me. “Thank you,” she said, her voice low and fierce. “Thank you for stopping me from marrying a liar.” She turned to her shell-shocked parents. “Daddy, please tell everyone the wedding is off. There will be no reception.”

She then, with a dignity that was heartbreaking to witness, walked back up the aisle alone, head high, and disappeared through a side door.

The original post asked if I was the asshole for planning this. The verdict is in, from both Reddit and life.

Mark: He’s been charged. He took a plea deal last week for a reduced sentence—probation, restitution, and a permanent felony record. His life is effectively cratered. He blames me for “overreacting.”

Chloe: We spoke once, briefly. She returned the ring, canceled everything, and is, in her words, “in therapy and thriving.” She’s started a small business with her would-have-been wedding savings. We’re not friends, but there’s a mutual respect. She knows I didn’t want to hurt her; I wanted to expose the truth she deserved to see.

Me: The $5,000 was just a fraction of the inheritance. My grandmother’s money wasn’t meant for legal battles and scrubbing a thief’s mold from my life. So, I used it as she intended. I paid the penalty to break my lease. I hired movers. I put down a deposit on a beautiful, light-filled one-bedroom apartment that is mine alone. The silence there isn’t just an absence of noise; it’s peace.

The final twist, the satisfying revenge that so many of you cheered for, wasn’t just seeing him led away in cuffs. It’s waking up every morning now, in my clean, quiet home, knowing I am free of him forever. I used my grandmother’s gift to build a happier life, just as she wished. And part of that happiness was ensuring the person who stole from me, both literally and figuratively, for years, would never be able to do it to anyone ever again.

So, Reddit, that’s the update. Was I the asshole? According to the court of public opinion and the actual court, no. But more importantly, according to the peace I feel every single day now, absolutely not.
