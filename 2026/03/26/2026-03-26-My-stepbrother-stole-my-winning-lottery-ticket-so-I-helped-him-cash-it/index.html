My stepbrother stole my winning lottery ticket, so I helped him cash it.

Posted on 2026-03-26 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Subreddit: r/ProRevenge
Title: TIFU by letting my stepbrother “borrow” $5 for gas.

If you ever need a masterclass in how a single, tiny act of kindness can unravel your entire life, look no further. My name is Jamie, and my fuck-up wasn’t some grand, dramatic mistake. It was a weary sigh on a Sunday evening, a fumble for my wallet, and the words, “Just pay me back, Kyle.”

Kyle is my stepbrother. The word conjures images of shared childhood memories, maybe some rivalry, but ultimately, family. With Kyle, it was always transactional. He was the human embodiment of a payday loan—high interest, hidden fees, and a guaranteed sense of regret. He was 25, perpetually “between ventures,” which meant he leeched off our dad, Steve, with the tenacity of a remora. Steve, my stepdad since I was six, was a good man, but blind where Kyle was concerned. He saw a charismatic go-getter; I saw a parasite in designer sneakers.

That Sunday, Kyle showed up at my apartment, reeking of cheap cologne and desperation. “Jamie, bro, crisis. My car’s on E, and I’ve got a job interview tomorrow across town. A real one. Can you spot me?”

I was tired. I’d just come back from a long run, my brain blissfully empty. I wanted a shower, not a Kyle. “How much?”

“Just twenty for gas. I’m good for it.”

We both knew he wasn’t. “I’ve got five in cash. Take it or leave it.” I pulled my wallet from my gym bag. Inside, amongst the frayed receipts and my ID, was a lottery ticket. Not just any ticket—a “Lucky Dip” I’d bought on a whim two days prior. The drawing was that night. It was a silly ritual, a $2 daydream. I’d scratched the “Lucky Dip” code but hadn’t checked the numbers online yet. It was my little secret hope, tucked behind a maxed-out credit card.

He took the five, his eyes doing that subtle, creepy scan of my open wallet. “Thanks, man. You’re a lifesaver.” He left, and I headed straight for the shower, the hot water washing away more than just sweat.

The next morning, chaos. My phone exploded. Missed calls, texts, notifications from the lottery app I’d forgotten I had. My heart hammered against my ribs as I opened it.

“CONGRATULATIONS! Your Lucky Dip ticket is a Grand Prize Winner! Estimated Jackpot: $2.3 Million.”

I choked on my own breath. This wasn’t happening. I scrambled to my dresser, where I’d left my wallet. My hands trembled as I opened it. The ticket was gone.

A cold, nauseating dread pooled in my gut. I knew. I knew. I called Kyle. Straight to voicemail. I drove to Dad’s house, my mind a hurricane of panic and fury.

I found them in the kitchen—Dad beaming, Kyle looking like the cat who swallowed the canary, and the goddamn winning ticket, now in a plastic protector, on the table between them.

“Jamie! Isn’t it incredible?” Dad boomed, pulling me into a hug. “Our Kyle, a millionaire!”

“Our Kyle?” I sputtered, pulling away. “Dad, that’s my ticket. I bought it. Kyle stole it from my wallet yesterday.”

Kyle’s face morphed into a mask of wounded indignation. “Whoa, whoa, Jamie. That’s a serious accusation. Why would you say that? I bought this myself. You know I play the same Lucky Dip sometimes.”

“You don’t have five bucks for gas, but you had two for a lottery ticket?” I shot back.

Dad looked pained. “Boys, please. Jamie, Kyle showed me the ticket, the receipt from the store…”

“Which store?” I demanded.

Kyle faltered for a second. “The QuikMart on 5th.”

“I bought mine at the Gas ‘N Go on Elm,” I said, a sliver of hope piercing through. “The transaction will be on my bank statement.”

Kyle didn’t even blink. “I paid cash.”

Of course he did. The argument escalated. I was “bitter,” “jealous of Kyle’s good fortune,” “always trying to bring him down.” Dad, my own dad, took Kyle’s side. The ticket, he said, was a blessing for Kyle, who needed it. I, with my stable IT job, was just being greedy. I left, devastated. The money was one thing. But my dad choosing Kyle’s obvious lie over me? That broke something fundamental.

The next week was a blur of despair. The lottery commission, seeing a dispute, put a hold on the payout. Kyle hired a slick lawyer. I was advised by a legal aid clinic that without concrete proof of purchase, my claim was weak. I was losing. Then, Kyle made his masterstroke.

He came to my apartment, not smug, but dripping with faux sympathy. “Look, Jamie, this is tearing the family apart. Dad’s a wreck. Let’s find a solution. Maybe… a split? 70/30? For the sake of family.”

I was so exhausted, so defeated, I almost considered it. Then he said, “I just want what’s fair. After all, blood is thicker than water, right?”

Something in his tone was off. Vicious. Pointed. He lingered, his eyes wandering around my small home office. On my desk, next to my monitor, was a sealed, legal-sized envelope. It had arrived a few days before the lottery drama, and in the chaos, I’d forgotten about it. It was from a private lab. Inside were the results of a paternity test.

My whole life, there’d been whispers. I didn’t look like Steve. My biological mother, who died when I was eight, had a… complicated past. A month ago, driven by these lifelong doubts, I’d sent in a test. Steve, unaware, had done one of those ancestry kits for fun. I used his public profile data for comparison. The envelope held my answer.

Kyle’s eyes locked onto that envelope like a hawk spotting a mouse. He didn’t know what it was, but he saw it was important, private, and I looked terrified that he’d seen it.

The next day, it was gone.

Two days later, I was summoned to Dad’s house. The atmosphere was glacial. Steve sat at the head of the table, looking older than I’d ever seen him. Kyle stood behind him, a hand on his shoulder.

“Jamie,” Steve began, his voice gravel. “Kyle showed me something. Something… devastating.” He slid a photocopied sheet across the table. It was the paternity test results. CONCLUSION: 0% PROBABILITY OF PATERNITY.

“He found this in your things,” Steve said, his eyes wet with betrayal. “After you accused him of theft. Were you going to use this to manipulate me? To get the ticket?”

My world tilted. Kyle had not only stolen it; he’d weaponized it. He’d framed me as the schemer, using a deeply personal secret as a weapon to solidify his theft.

“He stole that, too, Dad!” I cried, my voice cracking. “Just like the ticket! This was private! It was for me to know!”

“Why would you even get this, Jamie?” Steve’s question was a soft, heartbroken blow. “Unless you were planning something. Kyle says you’ve been resentful of our bond for years. That this,” he gestured to the results, “was your trump card. To prove you weren’t really my son, so you could take the money and leave.”

I looked at Kyle. He gave me a microscopic, triumphant smile. He had won. He’d taken my ticket, my proof of heritage, and my father, all in one ruthless play.

“Get out,” Steve whispered, then louder, “Get out of my house. And don’t come back. The lottery people will be hearing from Kyle’s lawyer. You have no claim to that money, and you… you are no son of mine.”

That was the nadir. The dark, airless pit. I lost everything that day. But hitting rock bottom has one advantage: it gives you a solid place to push off from.

I went to a real lawyer this time. Not legal aid. I sold my car, emptied my 401(k), and hired a shark named Evelyn Rossi. I laid it all out: the $5, the stolen ticket, the stolen paternity test, the defamation, the intentional infliction of emotional distress. “We’re not just suing for the ticket,” Evelyn said, her eyes sharp. “We’re suing for the destruction of your family. Conversion, fraud, slander, emotional distress. The whole parade.”

The lawsuit was nuclear. Kyle, thinking he was invincible, had already pre-spent chunks of his “winnings” – a new car, a Rolex, deposits on a condo. His lawyer was good, but Evelyn was better. She subpoenaed everything: Kyle’s bank records (showing no lottery ticket purchase), his phone location data (placing him nowhere near the QuikMart that day), and the security footage from my apartment building, which showed him entering my unit uninvited the day the paternity test went missing.

The pièce de résistance was the clerk from the Gas ‘N Go on Elm. Evelyn tracked her down. She remembered me, the “nice guy who always bought a Lucky Dip after his run.” She had no memory of Kyle.

During discovery, Kyle’s story crumbled. He contradicted himself. He got angry on the stand. He called me a “gold-digging bastard.” Evelyn just calmly entered another exhibit into evidence.

The trial was swift. The jury saw it all: the opportunistic theft, the calculated use of my deepest secret to turn my father against me, the premeditated fraud. They saw my father, Steve, sitting in the back row every day, growing paler and more shaken as the evidence mounted. He heard the security audio of Kyle bragging to a friend on the phone, “The old man will believe anything if you cry and call him Dad. The paternity thing was a lucky break; now Jamie’s finished.”

The verdict was unanimous. The court awarded me the full value of the lottery jackpot, plus interest. But more importantly, they awarded punitive damages for Kyle’s fraud and intentional infliction of emotional distress—a sum that dwarfed the lottery win. The judge, in her ruling, stated that Kyle’s actions were “a reprehensible pattern of deceit and malicious manipulation that struck at the very heart of family.”

Kyle wasn’t just ordered to pay. He was bankrupted. The cars, the watch, the condo deposits—all gone to satisfy the judgment. He was left with nothing but debt and a permanent mark on his record.

After the trial, I found Steve waiting for me on the courthouse steps. He looked broken. “Jamie,” he croaked, tears streaming down his face. “I am… so sorry. I was a fool. He played on my fears, on my love for him… Can you ever forgive me?”

It wasn’t a simple yes. The wound was deep. But I saw in his eyes the same devastation I’d felt. The loss wasn’t just mine. Kyle had stolen from him, too—his trust, his peace, his relationship with me. “It’s going to take time, Dad,” I said, the word feeling both foreign and right. “But we can start.”

He hugged me, a shuddering, full-bodied hug of regret and grief. Kyle was cut off completely—disinherited, evicted from Dad’s house, and reduced to a ghost in our lives.

The money? It’s in a trust. I still work my IT job. The vindication was the real prize. I used some of it to pay for family therapy for me and Steve. We’re rebuilding, slowly. The paternity result? It’s just a piece of paper. Steve is my dad in every way that matters. He’s the one who taught me to ride a bike, who held me at my mom’s funeral, who now looks at me with unwavering, remorseful love.

As for Kyle, the last I heard, he’s couch-surfing, telling anyone who will listen about how his family and the system screwed him over. He got his revenge, all right. He lit the fuse, not knowing he was strapped to the bomb. He got nothing. I got everything back, plus interest. And the $5 for gas? I finally got that back, too. The court made him repay it, with 9% interest. It’s framed in my office, a cheap reminder of the most expensive lesson my stepbrother ever taught me: sometimes, the best revenge is just letting a greedy man steal the rope to hang himself.
