My boss stole my 'last meal' before chemo, so I ruined his wedding.

Posted on 2026-03-08 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Title: AITA for letting my boss get arrested at his wedding after he stole my “last meal”?

Subreddit: r/AmItheAsshole

Body:

I know the title sounds unhinged. Please, let me explain. This isn’t just about a stolen lunch. It’s about the last straw on a back already broken by cancer, grief, and a man who thinks the sun shines out of his spreadsheet.

The antagonist, as you’d call him, is Vincent. Vincent isn’t just my boss; he’s the founder and CEO. He’s a man who micromanages the air we breathe. Ties too loud? Email. Two-minute bathroom break? Log it. Your personal smartphone, left face-up on your desk, is a “productivity hazard.” His mantra, drilled into us quarterly, is: “Nothing here is personal. Your ideas, your sweat, even the oxygen in this building is company property.” He said that. About the oxygen.

I’d been his executive assistant for seven years. Seven years of managing his whims, shielding the staff from his worst impulses, and quietly cashing out my unused vacation days—which he consistently denied but, due to a legal loophole in our state, had to pay out for if requested in a specific, obnoxious way. Every denied vacation, every “I need you this weekend,” I’d file the paperwork. The payouts went into a separate account. I’d named it, in a fit of dark humor, “Vengeance & Vitamins.” A fund for a future he’d forced me to contemplate.

Then, my present collapsed. Stage III. The brutal chemo regimen would start in a week. The weekend before D-Day, I did something sacred. I cooked my late mother’s rougaille saucisses—a Mauritian sausage and tomato stew she’d perfected. The smell of thyme, ginger, and garlic filled my apartment, a hug from the beyond. It was her “everything will be okay” meal. I made a large batch, packed a single, perfect portion in a glass container: my “last meal” before the chemicals would likely make eating anything a distant, nauseated memory. I brought it to work on Monday, a touchstone in the office fridge, a piece of home and her to get me through the day.

You know where this is going. Lunchtime came. My container was gone.

Panic, irrational and deep, set in. I asked around. Nothing. I went to Vincent, my voice shaking, explaining it was medically and emotionally significant. He gave me that flat, polished smile. “Lydia, surely you understand a shared fridge is a shared resource. Perhaps someone mistook it for a communal contribution. Don’t bring personal sentiment into the workspace.”

I went to Security. Marcus, the guard, saw my face—pale, pre-chemo, desperate. He broke protocol. We reviewed the footage from the kitchenette.

There he was. 11:47 AM. Vincent, in his $5,000 suit, opening the fridge. He paused, looked at my container, the only one not takeout. He took it. We followed the camera to his corner office. He ate it at his desk, scrolling through reports. He didn’t savor it; he consumed it, fuel for his next power move. Then, the final insult: he tossed the empty glass container—my mother’s container—into his trash bin. Not the recycling. The trash.

I felt a coldness then, a clarity that cut through the fear of cancer. This man had taken my time, my dignity, my oxygen, and now he’d taken my mother’s last embrace. He’d thrown her love in the garbage.

I quit on the spot. Emailed HR, carbon-copied him. Subject line: “Effective Immediately.” Body: “Due to the theft and consumption of my personal property, attached is security footage reference. My health necessitates this.”

He called, blustering. “Lydia, this is career suicide! It was just food!”

“It was my food,” I said quietly, and hung up.

At home, swimming in the dread of chemo and the acid of betrayal, I logged into my bank. “Vengeance & Vitamins.” The balance, built from seven years of denied beach trips and family Christmases, stared back. It was a specific, almost poetic number. Enough for a really, really good private investigator.

The PI’s name was Anya. I showed her the security footage. “I don’t want to sue him for the food,” I said. “I want to know if this,” I pointed at the man on screen, wiping my mother’s sauce from his lips, “is the tip of the iceberg. He believes everything is his for the taking. I want to know what else he’s taken.”

Anya’s eyes gleamed. “People like him,” she said, “they’re rarely just assholes in one dimension.”

The chemo started. It was brutal, as advertised. I was in the hospital, tethered to a rack of poisons meant to save me, when Anya called.

“Your boss,” she said, her voice crisp with triumph, “is an idiot. A greedy, arrogant idiot. He’s been skimming from the ‘employee wellness fund.’ He’s got a shell company invoicing for ‘corporate mindfulness seminars’ that never happened. He’s funneling it into his wedding. The cake, the band, the pre-nup for Bride Number Three—partially paid for by your colleagues’ stress-relief budget.”

It was so petty. So Vincent. Monetizing our well-being to fund his spectacle.

“What do you want to do with it?” Anya asked.

The nausea was cresting. I looked at the hospital wall, blank and sterile. I thought of my mom’s container in the trash. I thought of him saying “nothing is personal.”

“Make it personal,” I croaked. “Give it to the authorities. All of it. But… when is the wedding?”

“Saturday,” she said.

“Perfect,” I whispered. “Let it happen then.”

The wedding was a black-tie event at a modern art museum, streamed live—Vincent’s idea, so the “corporate family” could share his joy. From my hospital bed, my immune system in tatters, I pulled up the livestream. He looked smug, triumphant, in front of a geometric altar. Bride Number Three, half his age, gazed up at him like he’d invented money.

I had a project that day. My nurse, Sarah, had heard the story of the stolen rougaille. She’d gone to my apartment, retrieved the frozen leftovers, and brought them to the hospital kitchen. As the organist played, she warmed a portion for me.

On screen, the officiant asked, “Vincent, do you take this woman to be your lawfully wedded wife, to have and to hold, in sickness and in health…”

He opened his mouth. And that’s when the SWAT team moved in.

They weren’t subtle. They came in from the sides, tactical gear stark against the sea of chiffon and tuxedos. The officiant’s microphone picked up the commanding shout: “Vincent! Down on the ground! Now!”

The camera, unmanned, jerked and swayed, then focused, perhaps operated by a shocked guest with a sense of drama. It captured Vincent’s face perfectly: the smugness shattered, replaced by primal, bewildered terror. He sputtered, “What is the meaning of this? This is private property! This is my day!”

An officer was reading him his rights as they cuffed him. The words “embezzlement,” “wire fraud,” “grand larceny” cut through the whimpering of the crowd. Bride Number Three took one look at the man she was about to vow to cherish, now in zip-ties, and her eyes rolled back. She fainted clean away, collapsing in a heap of white silk, the livestream chat exploding into a frenzy of “OMG” and “Fake???”

I took a bite of the rougaille. The flavors exploded—ginger, garlic, tomato, the warmth of the sausages, the love woven into every spice by my mother’s hands. It was the best thing I’d ever tasted. A wave of warmth fought back the clinical chill of the hospital room.

Vincent, being led away in cuffs, spotted the camera. For a second, he stared directly into the lens, into the eyes of his unseen audience. And in that moment, I swear he knew. He saw the connection between the meal he’d thoughtlessly consumed and the handcuffs now ratcheted tight on his wrists. The personal had become inescapably professional.

The stream cut off. The room was quiet, save for the beep of my IV pump.

Sarah smiled. “Justice tastes good, huh?”

I smiled back, a real one, for the first time in weeks. “Better than revenge.”

So, Reddit, AITA? I used evidence of his crimes to have him arrested. But I timed it for maximum personal devastation, at the altar, live-streamed for all to see. I watched it while eating the very meal he stole, a meal that symbolized everything he’d ever taken from me and others. Was it a step too far? Or was it just the universe, with a little nudge from a vengeful, chemo-ridden former assistant, finally balancing the scales?
