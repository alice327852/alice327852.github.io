My Ex Moved In For Alimony, But Forgot About Mr. Whiskers' Strict Routine

Posted on 2026-03-08 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Subreddit: r/MaliciousCompliance
Title: My ex moved in to get more alimony. Now she’s trapped with “Mr. Whiskers’ Routine.”

If there’s one thing you should know about my ex-wife, Sarah, it’s that she has the moral flexibility of a gold-plated contortionist. Our divorce two years ago was a masterclass in her particular artistry. She’d married Potential, a mid-level project manager with a decent 401k. She’d divorced Actual, the same guy, because Actual hadn’t yet morphed into CEO-of-a-mansion. The settlement was brutal, but it got her out of my life. Or so I thought.

See, Sarah’s alimony was based on my old salary. Last month, I got promoted to Director of Systems Implementation. It came with a significant bump, a title that sounds important at parties, and, as I later learned from a gleeful text from a “mutual” friend, a direct wire into Sarah’s greed center. She saw dollar signs, not a man who’d worked 80-hour weeks for a decade.

Her petition to modify the alimony agreement hit my lawyer’s desk like a subpoena wrapped in perfume. I was prepared to fight it, to drag out the forensic accounting. What I wasn’t prepared for was coming home last Tuesday to find her Louis Vuitton suitcase in my foyer.

She was in the kitchen, pouring my expensive small-batch bourbon into a tumbler, as if the ghost of marriages past had taken up bartending. “Darling,” she said, without a hint of irony. “We need to talk.”

“You need to leave,” I countered, my voice flat.

“Here’s the thing,” she chirped, sipping my liquor. “My lease is up. My landlord is impossible. I’m, technically, temporarily homeless. And according to my lawyer, and your mortgage statements which list this as your primary residence, my need for suitable housing is a key factor in the adjustment hearing next week. If I’m here, experiencing the standard of living your new salary affords… well, it strengthens my case immeasurably. I’ve taken the guest room. Just until the hearing.”

It was a power play, brazen and calculated. She’d used her old key, which I’d forgotten to ask for back, a stupid oversight that now felt like a tactical surrender. If I threw her out, she’d cry to the judge about my cruelty, my lack of compassion. She’d paint me as a miser trying to hide my wealth from his “struggling” ex. My lawyer confirmed it: a messy, “he-said-she-said” eviction right now would look terrible.

That’s when I heard it. A soft, scratchy mrow from the hallway. Mr. Whiskers, my 18-year-old tuxedo cat, was peering blindly around the corner. His milky blue eyes, clouded by cataracts, were fixed in Sarah’s general direction.

Sarah followed my gaze and wrinkled her nose. “Ugh, that thing is still alive?”

A cold, placid calm settled over me. An idea, beautiful in its simplicity and diabolical in its execution, clicked into place. Mr. Whiskers wasn’t just a cat. He was a furry, diabetic, partially blind monument to routine. He was my responsibility, my beloved companion of fifteen years, and, as of this moment, my instrument of deliverance.

I forced a sigh, a mask of resigned defeat. “Fine, Sarah. You can stay. Temporarily. But the guest room is yours, and you stay out of my office. Understood?”

She looked like she’d just won the lottery. “Of course. See? Civilized.”

The next few days were a study in quiet warfare. She left makeup stains on the guest towels. She monopolized the living room TV with inane reality shows. She made pointed comments about the “dated” decor she herself had chosen. I smiled thinly and bided my time.

Then, on Friday, I dropped the bomb. “I have to go to Austin,” I announced, feigning stress. “Critical client project. System migration. I’ll be gone for two weeks. They’re flying me out tomorrow.”

Her face went through a fascinating spectrum: shock, suspicion, then dawning, greedy delight. Two weeks of unsupervised “experiencing my standard of living”? This was better than she’d dreamed.

“Oh,” she said, trying to sound concerned. “Well, that’s… unfortunate timing.”

“It is,” I agreed solemnly. Then I walked to the fridge and unpinned the most important document in the house. I placed it on the kitchen island with the reverence of a priest presenting a sacred text.

“This,” I said, “is Mr. Whiskers’ Routine.”

It was a laminated spreadsheet, color-coded, with footnotes. Sarah picked it up as if it were covered in something unpleasant.

5:00 AM: Wake-up yowl commences. Immediate feeding (Royal Canin Diabetic + 1/4 cup dry, 1/2 can wet – renal support formula) required to cease yowling. Food must be placed in the blue bowl, on the left side of the mat. He will not eat from any other bowl or location.
5:15 AM: Administer first dose of Cosequin (for joints) and Mirtazapine (appetite stimulant) crushed and mixed into the wet food. Must ensure he consumes all wet food to receive full dose.
7:00 AM: Insulin injection. Syringe pre-drawn and labelled in butter compartment of fridge. Technique demonstrated on orange. Subcutaneous, scruff of the neck. 3 units. NON-NEGOTIABLE.
12:00 PM: Mid-day wellness check. Refresh water. Clean litter box (World’s Best Cat Litter, unscented only). He may be napping in the sunbeam on the office chair; do not move him.
5:00 PM: Evening meal. Same as morning, but without Mirtazapine.
7:00 PM: Second insulin injection. NON-NEGOTIABLE.
10:00 PM: Final litter box check. “Dreamies” treat (maximum 3) if he used the box appropriately.

Below the schedule were warnings: “Deviations cause severe anxiety (his) and property damage (yours). Missed insulin = vet emergency = $2000+ bill. You are financially liable.”

Sarah’s face paled. “You can’t be serious. You expect me to be a… a catslave?”

“You insisted on living here, Sarah,” I said, my voice dripping with faux reasonableness. “This is part of the household. His health and well-being are my responsibility. Since you’re now part of the household, and I’m unavoidably called away, the responsibility transfers. It’s called cohabitation.” I leaned in. “My lawyer actually suggested it. Shows shared domestic burden. Looks great for the judge.”

I saw the conflict in her eyes: the revulsion versus the alimony jackpot. Greed won, as it always did. “Fine,” she snapped. “But I’m not cleaning that litter box more than once.”

“It’s your standard of living,” I shrugged.

The next morning, at 4:55 AM, I was already awake, packed, and listening. At 4:59, a low, guttural moan echoed through the hall. By 5:01, it had escalated into a sustained, soul-piercing yowl that sounded like a banshee being stepped on. I heard Sarah’s door slam. A thump. A hissed, “Shut UP!”

I quietly shouldered my bag and walked to the guest room door. I knocked softly. “Schedule, Sarah. 5 AM. He doesn’t stop. Ever.”

I left for my “flight” to the sound of her fumbling in the kitchen, cursing, and Mr. Whiskers’ operatic lament. I drove to a nearby extended-stay hotel, where I’d booked a room for two weeks. My home security system, with its discreet cameras in the living room, kitchen, and hallway, was now my favorite streaming service.

The first day was a symphony of failure. Sarah slept through the 7 AM injection. I watched on my phone as Mr. Whiskers sat forlornly by his empty blue bowl. At 8 AM, she stumbled out, poured some random kibble into a cereal bowl, and shoved it at him. He sniffed it and walked away. She cursed and went back to bed.

By day three, the dark circles under her eyes on the camera feed were glorious. She’d tried to lock him in the basement. The yowling became a horror movie soundtrack, amplified by concrete walls. She lasted two hours.

The insulin shots were the pinnacle of the comedy. I watched her, grimacing, trying to pinch a tuft of his fur with one hand while jabbing wildly with the other. Mr. Whiskers, patient soul, mostly just went limp. Half the time, I could see the liquid squirting back out onto his fur. She wasn’t even getting it under his skin.

She started talking to the cameras. “I know you’re watching, you bastard! This creature is a demon!”

I’d ordered his special food and insulin refills for delivery. She left the boxes by the front door for a day, until a notification from his automated feeder (which she’d unplugged) reminded her she’d be liable for vet bills.

The breaking point came on day ten. I was “in Austin,” attending very important virtual meetings in my hotel bathrobe. My phone buzzed with a motion alert. It was Sarah, in a silk robe, on the phone with her lawyer. Mr. Whiskers was weaving figure-eights around her ankles, yowling for his 5 PM meal.

“No, listen to me!” she was screeching. “I can’t think! I haven’t slept in ten days! The thing screams, it needs shots… I missed the hearing? WHAT DO YOU MEAN I MISSED THE HEARING?”

I leaned closer to the screen. Oh, this was beautiful. In her sleep-deprived, cat-harangued state, she’d forgotten the very court date she was here to leverage.

“I was up at five with the… I just… reschedule it! I can’t… he’s looking at me right now!” She was crying, actual tears of frustration. Mr. Whiskers, indifferent, headbutted her shin.

I decided it was time for my triumphant return. I packed up my hotel room, the picture of a weary business traveler.

When I let myself in, the smell hit me first. Not the litter box, which reeked, but the sour scent of neglect and despair. Sarah was on the couch, wrapped in a blanket, staring into the middle distance. She looked ten years older. Mr. Whiskers was sitting regally on the coffee table, his back to her, the picture of feline disapproval.

He heard my keys and turned, letting out a soft, chirpy brrp of recognition. I went to him first, scratching his favorite spot under the chin. “Hey, buddy. Did you hold down the fort?”

Sarah’s head swiveled toward me. The hatred in her bloodshot eyes was tempered by a profound, soul-crushing exhaustion. “You,” she rasped.

“Me,” I agreed cheerfully, picking up the laminated schedule from the floor. It was stained with what looked like coffee and resentment. “So. How was the routine?”

She didn’t answer. Just stared.

I opened my laptop, pulled up the security footage, and turned it toward her. A highlight reel of her misery played: her missing the 7 AM shot, her trying to feed him tuna from a can, her screaming into a pillow at 5:05 AM. The final clip was from that morning: her, asleep on the couch at 7:15 AM, the pre-drawn syringe still sitting untouched in the fridge.

“You neglected his medical needs, Sarah,” I said quietly, all pretense gone. “I have fourteen days of footage showing you failing to provide the basic care outlined in the schedule you agreed to. The schedule that is part of the ‘standard of living’ you so desperately wanted to experience. A judge would love this. Not just for the alimony hearing, but for animal neglect. It’s a liability thing.”

She looked from the screen to my face, the last of her fight evaporating. She was trapped, not by me, but by the inescapable, yowling, diabetic routine she’d arrogantly assumed she could ignore.

“What do you want?” The words were a defeated whisper.

“You drop the alimony modification. Full stop. We keep the original agreement.”
“And?”
“You leave. Today. You give me back the key. And you never, ever contact me again except through lawyers regarding the existing payment.”
She was silent for a long moment. Then, she nodded slowly, her eyes drifting to Mr. Whiskers, who was now purring contentedly in my lap, having received his proper 7 PM shot from a competent hand.

“Deal,” she breathed. “Just… get me away from the syringe.”

An hour later, her Louis Vuitton was back in her car. She didn’t look back as she drove away. The house was quiet, save for the soft rumble of Mr. Whiskers’ purr.

I cleaned the litter box, properly. I disinfected the kitchen. Then I opened a fresh can of his fancy wet food and gave him three extra Dreamies.

“You deserve it, pal,” I told him, stroking his soft, old head. “Best $200 I ever spent on a security system.”

He blinked his milky eyes slowly, as if to say he’d known how it would play out all along. Maybe he had. After all, he’s the one who wrote the routine. I just had the good sense to follow it.
