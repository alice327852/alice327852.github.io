My Rich Tenant Paid a Year's Rent in Cash. Then I Saw Him on Camera with a Garden Hose.

Posted on 2026-03-25 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

TIFU by renting to a trust fund brat… but the security footage let me win.

Subreddit: r/ProRevenge

Let me start by saying I’m not some slumlord. I own one duplex. It’s my childhood home. My parents left it to me and my sister, and we decided to keep it. She lives in one side; I live in the other. When she moved for her PhD two years ago, we decided to rent her side out. It’s a nice place—updated kitchen, good bones, fair rent for the area. I handle the landlord stuff. It’s been mostly smooth.

Enter Chad.

Not his real name, but it’s so fitting it hurts. Chad was 22, referred by a friend-of-a-friend. He showed up in a brand-new, obnoxiously loud BMW M4. Designer clothes, that perpetually bored, smug expression. His application was thin—no rental history, no real job—but he offered to pay a full year’s rent upfront, in cash. A year. His “references” were just his dad’s lawyer and his personal trainer. Big red flags, right? But my sister needed her share of the mortgage covered, and the sheer audacity of the cash offer made me think, Maybe it’ll be fine. Quiet money.

I should have listened to my gut. My gut was screaming.

The first two months were… tolerable. Loud music at odd hours, parking his Beamer so it blocked part of the shared driveway, the usual entitled stuff. Then, the kitchen sink developed a slow drip. I mean, slow. One drip every ten seconds. He texted me about it on a Friday evening: “Fix this. Now.” I was at work, an hour away. I told him I’d be there first thing Saturday morning.

Saturday morning, I’m there at 8 AM with my toolbox. Chad stumbles out, hungover, and says, “Don’t bother, man. I’ve got people coming over. Do it Monday.” I explained it was a simple washer replacement, ten minutes tops. He glared. “I said Monday. This is my place. You don’t just come in when you want.” The lease gave me right of entry for repairs, but to keep the peace, I left. I came back Monday after work. He wasn’t there. I fixed the drip in eight minutes flat.

I thought that was it.

A week later, I get the golden opportunity: a ten-day trip to Iceland with my oldest friends. A dream trip, saved for for years. The day before I leave, I get a text from Chad: “Bathroom sink is clogged. Unacceptable.” I’m packing, stressed. I run over. It’s not clogged. There’s a single, long blonde hair (not his) wrapped around the pop-up stopper. I remove it, water drains fine. I show him. He shrugs. “Took you long enough to get here.”

That should have been the second warning siren. It wasn’t.

I leave for Iceland. The first three days are pure magic: waterfalls, glaciers, geothermal pools. I’m disconnected, happy.

Then, on day four, my phone blows up. A series of texts from Chad, escalating in urgency:

Chad: Water pressure in my place is a joke. Fix it.
Me: (from the Blue Lagoon) I’m out of the country. Is there no water?
Chad: It’s a trickle. This is uninhabitable.
Me: Please check if the main shut-off valve under the kitchen sink is fully open. Sometimes it gets bumped.
Chad: Not my job. Send a plumber.
Me: Chad, I can’t magically send one from Reykjavík. Please check the valve. It’s a lever; just make sure it’s parallel to the pipe.

Silence for an hour. Then:

Chad: My bathroom is flooding.

My blood runs colder than the Icelandic wind. I call him. He doesn’t answer. I call my neighbor, Mrs. Gable, an elderly woman who lives behind us. “Honey,” she says, her voice worried, “I saw a young man carrying a big toolbox into your duplex earlier. And now there’s a lot of noise over there.”

A toolbox? Chad didn’t own a toolbox.

I’m panicking. I call an emergency plumbing service. They charge triple for after-hours, plus a “crisis dispatch” fee. It’s astronomical, but what choice do I have? Two hours later, the plumber calls me. His voice is grim.

“Sir, this is intentional damage. Someone took a pipe wrench to the main water supply valve behind the washing machine hookup. They didn’t just close it; they cranked it so hard they sheared the stem. Then, it looks like they took a hammer to the exposed pipe junction. Water was spraying directly into the wall cavity. There’s significant damage to the bathroom and the adjacent kitchen wall. This isn’t a leak; this is demolition.”

I feel sick. My vacation is over. I book the next flight home, a 36-hour odyssey of layovers and despair. The plumbing bill alone is five grand. The remediation company quotes another ten for water damage repair. My savings for the trip, gone, and then some.

I return to a nightmare. Chad’s side is a gutted mess of industrial dryers and exposed studs. He’s staying at a hotel, “billed to the landlord,” his daddy’s lawyer informs me via a scathing letter claiming constructive eviction and threatening a lawsuit for his “distress and inconvenience.”

I want to cry. I want to rage. I feel utterly defeated. Then, I remember the cameras.

Because my sister’s side was vacant before Chad moved in, we’d started a kitchen renovation. To keep an eye on the contractors, I’d installed a multi-camera security system—one in the living room, one facing the front door, one in the kitchen. They were small, discreet, Wi-Fi enabled. After Chad moved in, I meant to disable them, but with the trip coming up, I’d forgotten. I’d left them running, mostly offline, just recording to local SD cards. A stupid oversight. Or was it?

With a trembling hand, I pull the ladder into the living room. The camera is a small dome in a corner. I pop it open. The SD card is there. I check all four cameras. All have cards.

I take the cards to my computer. I fast-forward through days of nothing: Chad playing video games, Chad on the phone, Chad preening in the mirror. My heart sinks. Maybe he did it somewhere else, out of frame.

Then, I find it.

The timestamp is the afternoon of the “flood.” The camera in the living room has a partial view of the hallway leading to the bathroom/laundry area. Chad is on the phone, loud, bragging.

Chad: “Yeah, bro, I showed him. Landlord lesson 101. You don’t ignore me.”
Pause.
“Nah, I didn’t just clog a toilet. That’s amateur hour. I went for the main line. My dad’s company built a condo tower; I used to hang with the super. I know where to hit.”
Pause. He laughs, a nasty, self-satisfied sound.
“Turned off the main valve, then gave it a good whack with a wrench till it sheared. Then took a hammer to the coupling. Water went everywhere. Instant waterfall.”
Pause.
“He’s in Iceland or some shit. He’ll have to come back. Cost him thousands. That’ll teach him to prioritize my drips.”
Pause.
“His problem. My dad’s lawyers will eat him alive if he tries anything. I’ve got rights. He abandoned the property.”

He’s smiling into the camera, utterly pleased with himself.

I watch it ten times. The rage morphs. It crystallizes into something cold, sharp, and focused.

I make copies. I save backups in the cloud, on a USB drive, in my email. I go to the police station with the video file, the plumber’s report, and the remediation estimate. I file a report for criminal mischief and intentional destruction of property. The officer watching the video just shakes his head. “Trust fund baby having a tantrum,” he mutters.

The next day, I serve Chad. Not just an eviction notice—a criminal trespass notice and a civil suit for damages. I have a lawyer friend help me draft it. The notice is delivered to his luxury hotel by a process server, alongside the police report number.

The fallout is beautiful.

Chad’s daddy’s lawyer, a slick guy in a suit worth more than my car, shows up at my door. He tries the usual intimidation: “We will sue you for defamation, for illegal surveillance, for wrongful eviction. You will be buried in legal fees.”

I say nothing. I play the video on my laptop.

His face goes from smug to ashen. He stops me halfway through. “That… that is a private residence. You recorded him without consent. That’s inadmissible!”

I smile. “It’s my property. I installed them for renovation security and forgot to disable them. There’s no expectation of privacy in a common area, and the state is a one-party consent state for recording. But more importantly,” I lean forward, “it doesn’t matter if it’s admissible in some future hypothetical trial. It’s already done its job. The police have it. The eviction is based on observed lease violations and criminal activity. You want to fight this? Go ahead. We’ll take this video public. We’ll send it to your dad’s business partners. We’ll send it to the local news with the headline ‘Trust Fund Heir Destroys Rental For Fun.’ How’s that for a reputation?”

The bluster vanishes. He asks for a copy. I refuse. “Everything goes through my lawyer now.”

Two days later, a settlement offer arrives. They will pay for all repairs, in full, immediately. They will withdraw all threats and countersuits. In return, I drop the criminal charges and the civil suit. The eviction stands, non-negotiable.

I have one condition: Chad, in person, with his father present, comes to my house and apologizes to me and my sister. And he does it before the money changes hands.

They agree.

They show up in the dad’s Mercedes. Chad looks smaller. Deflated. His dad looks like he’s aged five years. Chad mumbles an apology his dad clearly wrote for him. It’s not satisfying in itself. The satisfying part is the sheer, profound shame radiating from his father, a man who finally saw the monster he’d created.

The money hits my account. The repairs are finished. Chad is gone.

I never went back to Iceland. I used the leftover settlement money to take my sister on a long weekend to the coast. We sat on a beach, listening to the waves, and I felt a deep, quiet peace.

The cameras? They’re still up. And now, they’re very clearly mentioned in the lease. With a highlighted clause about intentional property damage resulting in criminal prosecution.

Sometimes, the universe forgets to be just. So you have to help it remember. All it took was one entitled brat, one moment of vicious stupidity, and one forgotten SD card to make it right.
