He stole my lottery ticket on a date, so I set the perfect trap.

Posted on 2026-04-06 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

TIFU by trusting my “nice guy” date…who stole my winning lottery ticket.

Reddit, I am vibrating with a mixture of residual fury, profound vindication, and the kind of life lesson that leaves a permanent mark on your soul. Buckle up. This is a saga of misplaced trust, a life-changing scratch-off, and the sweet, sweet taste of malicious compliance.

Let’s rewind to last Friday. I (29F) had a first date with Mark (31M), a guy I’d met through a mutual friend’s BBQ. He was… fine. Pleasant. The conversation wasn’t electric, but it was easy. He was painfully earnest, the kind of guy who, within the first hour, made sure to tell me he “wasn’t like other guys.” He held doors, asked thoughtful questions, and listened intently. He was, on paper, a Nice Guy™. We had drinks, shared some decent tapas, and parted with a polite hug. He texted later saying he’d had a great time and hoped to see me again. I was on the fence, but thought, Why not? A second date couldn’t hurt.

Saturday night, date two. A casual movie and then coffee. Again, it was… fine. He was a little more talkative, a little more insistent on his own virtues. “I just believe in treating women with respect, you know? It’s so rare these days.” I nodded, sipping my latte, feeling a faint itch of condescension but brushing it off. After coffee, he walked me back to my apartment building. It was a chilly night, and I’d stupidly worn a light jacket.

“I’m just going to run upstairs and grab my warmer coat,” I said, standing in the brightly lit, slightly shabby lobby. “Do you mind waiting here? I’ll just be a minute.”

“Of course!” he said, giving me that earnest, head-tilted smile. “Take your time. I’m happy to wait.”

Here comes the FU, in all its glorious, sleep-deprived stupidity. As I was digging for my keys, I also pulled out the contents of my pockets: a crumpled receipt, a lip balm, and a scratch-off lottery ticket I’d bought on a whim earlier that day. A $5 “Lucky 7s” ticket. I’d been carrying it around, utterly forgetting to scratch it. In my rush, I placed it on the small, circular table in the center of the lobby, next to a sad-looking fake plant, to free my hands. I found my keys, gave Mark another smile, and sprinted for the elevator.

The whole trip took maybe four minutes. Tops. I burst back into the lobby, warmer jacket on, to find Mark standing exactly where I’d left him, hands in his pockets, rocking slightly on his heels.

“Ready?” he asked cheerfully.

I grabbed my purse, and my eyes fell on the table. The ticket was gone.

A cold trickle of confusion ran down my spine. “Hey, did you see my lottery ticket? I left it right here.”

Mark’s face morphed into a picture of innocent concern. “Lottery ticket? No, I didn’t see anything. Maybe it fell?” He made a show of looking around his feet, even lifting the edge of the ugly area rug.

I got on my hands and knees, checking under the table. Nothing. The lobby was empty except for us. A deep, unsettling feeling began to churn in my gut.

“It was right here, Mark. I literally put it down four minutes ago.”

He straightened up, his expression shifting to one of gentle, wounded reproach. “Are you… are you suggesting I took it? A five-dollar lottery ticket?” He let out a small, hurt laugh. “Come on, [My Name]. I’m not that kind of guy. You know that. I’m not like other guys who would do something so… petty.”

His performance was masterful. The slight tremor in his voice, the way he looked genuinely pained that I could suspect him. It was so convincing that, for a moment, I doubted myself. Had I actually taken it upstairs? Had it blown away in some phantom lobby breeze? I muttered an apology, the confusion now laced with embarrassment. We parted ways outside, the date ending on a massively awkward note. He texted me later: “I’m really sorry about the misunderstanding tonight. I hope you find your ticket. I’d never do anything to violate your trust.” I replied with a terse “Thanks.”

The next morning, still feeling weird about the whole thing, I remembered the lottery app on my phone. You could scan your tickets to check winners. I had the receipt from where I’d bought it. On a whim, I entered the serial number.

A spinning wheel. A pause.

$10,000.

The world dropped out from under me. I stared at the screen, my blood turning to ice, then fire. Ten. Thousand. Dollars. The ticket I’d left on the table. The ticket that had vanished. The ticket Mark, with his wounded, earnest eyes, had sworn he never saw.

Coincidence? No. Hell no.

I felt a rage so pure and hot it scared me. But rage alone doesn’t get you ten grand back. I needed proof. And then I remembered: the lobby security camera. A single, dusty dome camera in the corner, pointed right at the lobby table. I’d never given it a second thought.

I marched down to the super’s office. Sal, a man in his 60s who communicates primarily in grunts and smells vaguely of cigar smoke and damp concrete, listened to my frantic story, his expression unchanging.

“I need to see the footage from last night, Sal. Please. It’s really important.”

He took a long sip of coffee from a chipped “World’s Best Grandpa” mug. “No can do. Building policy. Footage only goes to the police. You got a crime, you call the cops. They come with a paper, I give them the DVD.”

“Sal, it’s me! I live in 4B! I’ve lived here for five years!”

“Policy,” he grunted, turning back to his racing form.

Frustration threatened to swallow me whole. But then, clarity. Sal wasn’t being a dick; he was following rules. And rules, I realized, could be my weapon.

Malicious compliance began to whisper in my ear.

I went back upstairs. I didn’t call Mark. I didn’t scream or accuse. Instead, I called the non-emergency police line and filed a formal report for grand larceny. I explained the situation calmly: a valuable item (the winning ticket, valued at $10,000) was stolen from a private building lobby. I provided Mark’s full name, phone number, and the time of the incident. The officer on the phone was professional, but I could hear the slight skepticism—a lovers’ quarrel over a lottery ticket? Still, a crime was a crime.

Two days later, a detective called. “We obtained the security footage from your building superintendent,” she said, her voice now sharp with interest. “It’s very clear. The individual you identified waits until the elevator doors close, looks around, picks up the ticket from the table, examines it, and pockets it. He then resumes his original position, acting as if nothing happened.”

The vindication was a physical wave, leaving me weak-kneed. They had him. I had him.

The detective informed me they would be contacting Mark. They advised me not to. But the malicious compliance wasn’t complete. He’d stolen from me, then gaslit me, playing the wounded gentleman. The social facade was his shield. I decided to remove it.

I didn’t post the footage. That felt too aggressive, and possibly legally dicey. Instead, I took a screenshot of the police report case number (blurring my personal info). I went to my Instagram story—where we had several mutual friends from that original BBQ—and I wrote a calm, devastating post.

It said: “An update to the weird situation from last weekend. Sadly, the lottery ticket I misplaced was a $10,000 winner. Even more sadly, the police have confirmed it was stolen by someone I trusted. The case number is [XXXXX]. I’m so incredibly sad that Mark [Last Name] felt he had to do this. I truly believed he was a good person. If anyone has any information, please contact the [Town] Police Department. I hope he gets the help he needs.”

I tagged no one. I just posted it and waited.

The detonation was immediate. Our mutual friends’ group chat exploded. Screenshots of my story flooded in, followed by texts to me: “OH MY GOD,” “Are you serious??” “I always got a weird vibe from him!” Within an hour, Mark’s social media profiles went private. Then, they disappeared altogether.

The police moved quickly. With the footage and the documented value, they arrested him. He confessed almost immediately, apparently crumbling under the dual pressure of the law and the total collapse of his social reputation. He claimed it was a “moment of madness,” that he saw the ticket, scratched it “as a joke,” and when he saw it was a winner, panic and greed took over. He’d cashed it at a convenience store a twenty-minute drive away, thinking he’d gotten away with it.

But he hadn’t counted on Sal’s rigid policy. He hadn’t counted on my refusal to just be a polite, accommodating victim. He hadn’t counted on malicious compliance.

The restitution process began. Because he’d been charged and convicted (he took a plea deal for felony theft), the court ordered him to pay me the full $10,000, plus fees. I got every cent. The final, beautiful twist? Part of his sentence involved being registered in a database for lottery fraud. He’s now officially a known lottery ticket thief, which means he’s banned from claiming prizes over a certain amount in our state. A permanent, public mark on his record.

The fallout was deeply satisfying. The mutual friend who introduced us apologized profusely. The broader social circle now uses “pulling a Mark” as shorthand for someone being a fake-nice, secretly shitty person. And I? I learned a brutal lesson about trust, performance, and the importance of following proper procedure.

I also learned that sometimes, the “nice guy” isn’t just boring. Sometimes, he’s a thief who will look you in the eye and lie while your life-changing money is in his pocket. And sometimes, the sweetest revenge isn’t screaming or fighting—it’s calmly handing the problem to the police, letting the system work, and watching as someone’ meticulously constructed, “not-like-other-guys” persona shatters under the weight of a single, undeniable frame of security footage.

Thanks for the life lesson, Mark. And thanks for the $10,000. I donated the first $5 to a local women’s shelter, in your name. Consider it my own act of maliciously compliant philanthropy.
