My Roommates Stole My Late Mom's Cat. As a Paralegal, I Knew Exactly How to Handle It.

Posted on 2026-04-06 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Subreddit: r/pettyrevenge
Title: LITTER-ally stole my dead mom’s cat. I made them homeless in 10 minutes.

The silence after a parent dies isn’t really silent. It’s filled with the ghost of their voice in the hallways, the echo of their laugh, and the heavy, suffocating weight of everything they left behind. For me, that weight was two things: $5,000 in a checking account I couldn’t bring myself to touch, and Mochi, my mom’s ancient, grumpy, glorious Persian cat.

Mochi was seventeen, a flat-faced cloud of white fur with permanently judgmental aqua eyes. My mom had him since I was in middle school. He was her shadow, her confidante, her little king. Inheriting him felt like being handed a piece of her soul, one that required twice-daily eye wipes and expensive renal-support kibble. I moved him into my apartment, a decent two-bedroom I shared with my roommate, Kieran.

Kieran had been a friend-of-a-friend, a seemingly okay guy when we’d moved in a year ago. But six months ago, he got fired from his retail job for “attitude issues” and decided unemployment was his creative era. His side of the apartment became a monument to lethargy: pizza boxes stacked like modern art, a persistent sour milk smell, and a constant, low hum of video game gunfire. He stopped paying his half of the rent on time, always with a sheepish, “You’ll cover me, right? I’ll get you back.” My grief made me passive. I just wanted quiet. I paid.

Mochi hated Kieran. He’d hiss if Kieran walked by too quickly, which was the closest thing to exercise Kieran did. I took it as a sign of the cat’s good judgment. My routine became work, caring for Mochi, and trying to ignore the growing landfill in the living room. The $5,000 sat in my account. I thought maybe I’d use it for Mochi’s vet bills, or a deposit on a solo place when the lease was up. It was my last tangible connection to my mom’s care for me. Untouchable.

Kieran found out about the money because he went through my mail. I came home to find a bank statement open on the counter.

“Five grand, huh?” he said, not looking up from his game. “From mommy?”

The casual cruelty stole my breath. “That’s private, Kieran.”

“Whatever. Listen, that’s perfect. You can give me that as back pay.”

I blinked. “Back pay?”

“For putting up with you,” he said, finally pausing the game and turning to me. His face was smug. “For the last six months, man. The moping, the sadness, the fucking cat hair everywhere. I’ve been doing emotional labor. That money’s basically mine.”

The anger that rose in me was so hot and clean it felt like a fever. “You haven’t paid rent in full for six months. You live in a biohazard. You don’t get to bill me for my grief.”

He shrugged, the picture of nonchalance. “Think about it. I deserve that money way more than you do. Or that ancient cat.”

That was the first red flag. I started sleeping with Mochi in my room, the door locked.

The retaliation came a week later. I worked as a paralegal, sometimes pulling long hours. One evening, I came home to find Kieran sitting at the kitchen table like a shabby king holding court.

“We need to talk about the cat,” he announced.

“Mochi is fine.”

“Is he? You’re gone 9, 10 hours a day. That’s neglect. I’ve been reading about animal welfare.” He leaned forward. “It’s not right. I’m thinking of calling the ASPCA. Or just taking him to a shelter. They’ll find him a better home.”

My blood ran cold. “If you touch my cat, I will end you.” The words were quiet, deadly serious.

He just smirked. “Then pay up. The inheritance for the cat’s safety. Simple.”

I knew then I couldn’t reason with him. He’d sunk from lazy mooch to aspiring extortionist. I went to my room, shaking, clutching a purring Mochi to my chest. That night, I didn’t sleep. I made a plan.

The next morning, I called my landlord, Mr. Davies. He was a no-nonsense retired contractor. I laid it all out: the late rent (I had texts and venmo requests as proof), the harassment, the threats against my pet. I was calm, professional, citing clauses in our lease about quiet enjoyment and tenants’ rights to safety. I asked, hypothetically, what the process would be if I needed to break my lease due to a hostile living situation.

“You got documentation?” Mr. Davies asked, his voice gravelly.
“I do.”
“Bring it by my office. We’ll talk.”

I went on my lunch break. I showed him everything. The proof I’d covered Kieran’s rent, the photos of the filth, and a recorded audio clip (single-party consent state) of Kieran saying, “The cat’s mine now. Pay me or you’ll never see him again.” I’d recorded our last “talk” just in case.

Mr. Davies’s face hardened. “This is extortion. And a threat of property theft. Your pet is considered property. This is a breach of lease on his part, multiple times over.” He drafted a formal notice of lease violation for Kieran, citing non-payment, unsanitary conditions, and harassment of a co-tenant. He gave me a copy. “If he escalates,” Mr. Davies said, sliding a second document toward me, “this is a 30-day notice to vacate, just for him. I’ll sign it as needed. You’re a good tenant. I don’t put up with this crap.”

I had the nuclear codes in my bag. I just needed the right moment.

It came two days later. A filing got postponed at work, freeing me up by 3 PM. I decided to surprise Mochi with some early cuddle time. As I pushed open the apartment door, the scene in the living room froze the very air in my lungs.

Kieran was there, with my mom’s old, hard-shell pet carrier—the one I kept tucked in my closet. Mochi’s fancy kibble bag was next to it, along with his favorite plush donut bed and his medication.

Kieran was stuffing a bag of treats into the carrier.

Mochi was nowhere in sight.

“What are you doing?” My voice was eerily calm.

He jumped, then recovered, that sickening smirk plastering his face. “Oh, hey. Just packing up my emotional support animal. My therapist says I need one. Mochi’s bonded with me.”

“Like hell he has,” I spat, my eyes darting around. “Where is he?”

“Safe,” Kieran sang. He walked over, standing too close. “Here’s the deal. You transfer that $5,000 to me right now. I take Mochi and his stuff and I go. You never see us again. You refuse…” He patted the carrier. “I take him to a shelter three counties over. You’ll never find him. An old cat like that? He probably won’t last a week.”

The rage was gone, replaced by a glacial, focused certainty. This was the moment.

“So, it’s extortion, then?” I said, slowly setting my work bag down on the cleanest part of the couch. “You’re stealing my inherited cat to force me to give you my inherited money.”

“Dramatic much? It’s compensation. Now, pay up.”

“Okay,” I said, pulling out my phone. “Let me just… make a call to confirm something first.”

I didn’t call the bank. I called Mr. Davies, putting it on speaker.

He answered on the second ring. “Davies.”

“Mr. Davies, it’s me. I’m here in my apartment with my roommate, Kieran. He is currently in the process of packing up my cat, Mochi, into a carrier. He has just stated, and I quote, ‘You transfer that $5,000 to me right now or I take the cat to a shelter and you’ll never see him again.’ He is demanding money in exchange for not stealing my property. Is that grounds for the action we discussed?”

Kieran’s face went from smug to confused to pale in a three-second span.

Mr. Davies’s voice crackled through the speaker, loud and clear. “That is extortion and attempted theft. Yes, it is. Mr. Kieran, this is your landlord. Based on recorded lease violations of non-payment, unsanitary conditions, and now this criminal harassment, your tenancy is hereby terminated. You have 24 hours to vacate the premises. A formal notice is already drafted. If you are not gone with all your belongings by 6 PM tomorrow, I will accompany the police for a formal eviction, which will stay on your record. Do you understand?”

Kieran looked like a fish gasping on a dock. “You… you can’t… you set me up!”

I walked over to my bag, pulled out the 30-day notice Mr. Davies had pre-signed, and held it up. “Actually, he just upgraded it to 24 hours. Consider yourself lucky. Now, where is Mochi?”

“Screw you!” Kieran shouted, making a lunge for the carrier.

“If you take one step with that carrier,” I said, my voice low, “I am dialing 911 and you will be arrested for felony extortion right now. The recording is already with my landlord. Where. Is. My. Cat.”

Deflation is a physical thing. I watched his shoulders collapse. He pointed a trembling finger toward his bedroom. “Under… under the bed. He hissed at me.”

I didn’t wait. I strode into his disgustingly foul room, got on my knees, and peered into the darkness. Two glowing aqua eyes stared back. “Come on, Your Majesty. Time to go.”

Mochi, bless his vengeful little heart, sauntered out, gave a disdainful flick of his tail at the room, and trotted to my bedroom. I shut the door safely behind him.

When I returned, Kieran was sputtering. “You can’t do this! I have rights!”

“You forfeited them,” Mr. Davies’s voice came from the phone, which I’d set on the table. “Be out by 6 PM tomorrow. Any damage to the property will be taken from your security deposit, which is already forfeit for unpaid rent. Goodbye.” The line went dead.

I picked up my phone. “I’m going out for a few hours. I suggest you start packing. When I return, if you’re still here, I call the police. If anything of mine, including my cat, is disturbed, I call the police.”

I took Mochi, his carrier, and his things, loaded them into my car, and drove to a pet-friendly café. I bought a latte and watched Mochi nap in his carrier, his little world safe again. Then I called Mr. Davies back.

“He’s packing?”
“He will be. Sir, would it be possible to change the locks tomorrow? I’ll pay for it.”
“I’ll meet you there at 5:30. We’ll do it together.”

The next day, I took a personal day. From my car parked down the street, I watched Kieran, looking ragged and defeated, haul garbage bags of his stuff to a waiting Uber XL at 4 PM. The moment the car turned the corner, Mr. Davies’s truck pulled up.

We changed the locks together. Then, we entered the apartment. The smell was… improved, but the sheer volume of crap Kieran had left behind—actual garbage, broken furniture, mildewed clothes—was staggering.

“This goes beyond security deposit,” Mr. Davies grumbled. “I’ll bill him for junk removal.”

With his permission, I helped. We bagged up every last vestige of Kieran’s existence. The pizza-box skyscrapers, the crusted dishes, the sad, stained gaming chair. We carried it all down to the curb, a monument to laziness and entitlement now waiting for sanitation.

By 6:05 PM, it was done. The new keys were in my hand. The apartment, though still needing a deep clean, was mine. The silence was back, but this time it was peaceful. It was just me and the ghost of my mom’s laugh, and the soft, wheezy purr of her sleeping cat, curled up on the freshly vacuumed couch.

I ordered takeout, opened a window to let in the clean evening air, and sat next to Mochi. I scratched behind his ears, the way my mom used to. He bumped his head against my hand.

The $5,000 was still in my account. Maybe I’d use it for a proper memorial for my mom. Or maybe, just maybe, Mochi and I would use it for a deposit on a nicer, sunnier place with no roommates at all.

But for tonight, the petty, perfect revenge was enough. Kieran was homeless, his stuff was on the curb, and my king was safe on his throne. Sometimes, justice is a quiet click of a new lock, and the sound of a garbage truck arriving right on schedule.
