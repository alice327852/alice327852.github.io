My husband spent our baby fund on a PS5. So I played the long game.

Posted on 2026-04-07 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Title: My Husband Bought a PS5 with Our Baby Fund Money. I Faked a Pregnancy to Burn It All Down.

Subreddit: r/relationship_advice

Post:

Hey Reddit. I don’t actually need advice. It’s all over. I’m in a new apartment, sipping wine that I absolutely should not be drinking if I were actually pregnant, and feeling a mix of emptiness and a savage, quiet triumph. I just need to scream this story into the void, because the people in my life right now are either blocked or getting a heavily sanitized version. So here’s the raw, unfiltered truth.

My husband (Mark, 34) and I (32) have been together since college. A decade. A decade of shared dreams, inside jokes, building a life. The “trying for a baby” chapter was supposed to be the most exciting one. We’d even opened a joint high-yield savings account, cringingly labeled “Baby Fund” in the bank app. Every month, we’d both auto-deposit a set amount. It was sacred.

Then, about six months ago, the “working late” started. First it was once a week. Then twice. Then it was every Thursday, like clockwork, and random other nights. “Crunch time on the new project,” he’d say, smelling vaguely of some new cologne I didn’t buy him. My gut, that primal, nauseating alarm bell, started clanging. I’d check his phone when he showered—nothing. He’d become secretive with it, angled away, password changed from our anniversary to something else. I felt like a cliché, the paranoid wife, but the feeling was a living thing coiled in my stomach.

The first real crack appeared three weeks ago. I was reconciling our credit card statement (a shared duty he’d suddenly “been too busy” to help with) and saw a $700 charge from a major electronics retailer. The memo field, a punchline written by the universe itself, read: “Baby Supplies.”

My blood went cold. Then hot. Baby Supplies. A PS5, to be exact. I knew because the retailer’s code was unmistakable, and a quick Google of the charge amount confirmed it. I sat at the kitchen table, the same table where we’d planned our future, and felt the foundation of my life splinter.

I confronted him that night, the statement printed out and placed between us like a declaration of war. “Mark, what is this?”

He didn’t even flinch. He sighed, a long-suffering sound. “Babe. It’s for my nephew, Timmy. His birthday is next month. I used the joint card because mine was maxed, and I just forgot to pay it back. I must have fat-fingered the memo.” He reached for my hand. “You’ve been so stressed with the baby stuff. You’re getting hormonal, reading into things.”

Hormonal. Paranoid. The two words every gaslit woman knows by heart. He made me feel small, crazy, for doubting him. For a night, I almost believed him. Almost.

But the $700 never got paid back. The “Baby Fund” was lighter by exactly that amount. And my gut kept screaming.

So, I decided to play a game of my own.

I bought a box of cheap ovulation tests and, after a YouTube tutorial, used a drop of luteinizing hormone supplement and a red Sharpie to engineer the faintest second line. It looked real. It had to.

I waited for a quiet Sunday morning. I left the test on the bathroom counter and called him in, my voice trembling with a performance of hope. “Mark… look.”

He stared. His face didn’t light up. It didn’t crumble with joy. It… froze. Then a horrible, paled mask of something like guilt and panic slid over his features. He forced a smile, a brittle, awful thing. “That’s… wow. That’s great, honey!” He hugged me, his body stiff. No tears, no spinning me around, none of the reactions we’d joked about for years. Just hollow congratulations and a distant, shell-shocked look in his eyes.

The hook was set.

I leaned into the role. “We need to get serious about savings,” I announced the next day, all business. “The doctor said with my age, we have to be prepared for anything. We need to tighten the belt. No more big, unnecessary purchases.”

I watched the color drain from his face. “How much is in the Baby Fund right now?” he asked, trying to sound casual.

I pulled up the app. “Well, it was at our target, but after some recent… reallocations, it’s a bit behind.” I stared right at him. “Maybe you should return that PS5 for Timmy? Get him a gift card instead. We need every penny.”

He got defensive instantly. “I can’t return it! It’s… already wrapped. At my brother’s place. Don’t worry, I’ll cover it from my bonus.”

A lie. His bonus wasn’t due for months. The panic in his voice was the confirmation I needed. He was protecting that console, but not for a nephew.

The next day, while he was at work (or so he said), I checked his car. Our spare key fob still worked. In the trunk, under a blanket and a spare tire he’d never changed, I found it. The PS5, box pristine. Nestled beside it, like a sick diorama of my failing marriage, was a keycard from a boutique hotel downtown and a sample vial of perfume—something sweet and cloying, nothing I would ever wear.

The pieces clicked into place with a sound that felt like bones breaking. The late nights. The new cologne to cover her scent. The hotel. The perfume sample, a forgotten token. The PS5, bought with money meant for our child, probably to install in his “bachelor pad” crash pad with her.

I closed the trunk. The sadness was a thick, cold tar, but on top of it, now, was a clear, sharp fury. I needed proof he couldn’t talk his way out of.

I hired a PI. The best I could afford, using money from a secret savings account my mom had told me to always keep. “For emergencies,” she’d said. This qualified. While I fake-dry-heaved into the toilet each morning for Mark’s benefit, the PI followed him. For two weeks, I lived a double life: the hopeful, nauseated expectant mother, and the cold, calculating detective of my own life.

The email from the PI came with a PDF attachment. I opened it in my locked office at work. There they were. Mark and a woman I recognized from his company holiday party—Sasha from Marketing. Holding hands leaving the hotel. Kissing in a parked car at a scenic overlook. Laughing over dinner at the intimate Italian place he’d told me was for client dinners. The photos were timestamped, crystal clear. The final one was of them walking into an apartment building together, his arm around her waist, carrying a bag from that same electronics store.

He wasn’t just cheating. He was living a double life, funded in part by our dream of a family.

The grief was immense, but it was now secondary to the mission. Survival. I quietly found a new apartment, paid the deposit, set up a mail forward. I met with a lawyer, just a consultation, to understand my rights. I separated my direct deposits into a new, personal account. I waited for my moment.

It came with his text: “The guys and I are doing an overnight fishing trip this weekend! Need some guy time before the baby comes :)”

The smiley face felt like a blade. I knew “the guys” was a 5’7” blonde with a perfume sample.

Saturday morning, I helped him pack his “fishing gear” (new hiking clothes, the cologne). I kissed him goodbye, my performance of loving, trusting wife now a twisted Oscar-worthy farce. The second his car turned the corner, I moved.

I packed my essentials: documents, keepsakes from my family, my favorite books, my laptop. I left the wedding photos, the shared gifts, the ugly vase his mother gave us. I went to the bank and withdrew exactly half of every remaining shared account. Not a penny more, not a penny less. It was mine. The Baby Fund, now a grotesque joke, I drained completely. Let him explain that to his accountant.

Then, I set the stage on our kitchen counter.

I placed the fake positive pregnancy test, now a prop in this tragedy. Next to it, I laid out the PI’s photos in a neat, damning fan. Not all of them, just the clearest three: the kiss, the hand-hold leaving the hotel, him entering her apartment building.

Finally, I wrote the note. I didn’t agonize over it. The words came cold and easy.

Mark,
The baby was fake. The PS5 was a fake reason to drain our future. It seems we were both living in a fantasy. Here’s your reality.
Enjoy your real ones.
Do not contact me. My lawyer will be in touch.

I didn’t sign it. I didn’t need to.

I walked out of the house that held a decade of my life, drove to my new, sparse, quiet apartment, and turned off my phone. I had a new SIM card ready. I went full ghost protocol. Blocked him on every platform—phone, email, social media. Blocked his family, his friends, anyone who could be a flying monkey.

He found out I was gone, of course, when his “fishing trip” ended and he returned to a half-empty house and a gallery of his betrayal on the counter. My old phone, left in a drawer, eventually lit up with hundreds of notifications. Texts ranging from confused (“Where are you? We need to talk!”) to angry (“How could you do this? You lied!”) to desperate (“Please, I’m so sorry, it meant nothing!”).

Our mutual friends have been messaging me on Instagram, confused, saying he’s a wreck, that he’s claiming I had a “mental break” and stole money. I haven’t responded to a single one. They’ll get the truth when the divorce papers are served. Until then, let him twist. Let him explain the photos. Let him account for the missing “Baby Supplies” PS5 to his new girlfriend when he can’t take her to nice hotels anymore.

I am alone. I am heartbroken. The future I dreamed of is ash. But I am also, for the first time in months, in control. I am not hormonal. I am not paranoid. I am the consequence.

TL;DR: Husband used our baby savings to buy a PS5 while cheating. I faked a pregnancy to expose his panic, hired a PI to confirm the affair, took my half of everything, and left the evidence for him to find. I’ve vanished completely. He’s lost his mind trying to find me. The silence is my revenge.
