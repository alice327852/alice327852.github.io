He Canceled My Dream Solo Trip, So I Took a Trip to His Mom’s House

Posted on 2026-04-09 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Subreddit: r/ProRevenge
Title: My “Nice Guy” Date Canceled My Vacation, So I Used My Winning Lotto Ticket for Malicious Compliance

The $50,000 lottery ticket felt like a tiny, crumpled rectangle of karma. I’d never won anything before, not even a free coffee. After the initial, scream-into-a-pillow shock, a serene clarity settled over me. For the first time in years, I could breathe without the weight of student loans and car repairs and “just getting by” pressing down on my sternum. The money wasn’t life-changing in the “quit my job forever” sense, but it was freedom-changing. And I knew exactly what to do with it: two weeks in Iceland. The land of fire, ice, and solitude. A solo dream vacation I’d been curating in a secret Pinterest board for half a decade.

Enter Mark. We’d met at a friend’s board game night. He was quiet, polite, laughed at my terrible dice rolls. He seemed… safe. We went for coffee. Then dinner. Then a movie. Three dates. That’s all it was. But for Mark, a self-professed “Nice Guy,” three dates were apparently a blood pact.

The love-bombing started subtly. “I’ve never felt this kind of connection before,” he’d say, staring a little too intently over his latte. “We’re just so in sync.” By the third date, he was talking about “soulmates” and how his mother would adore me. It was a whirlwind of supposed compliments that felt less like admiration and more like ownership. I was already pulling back, crafting the gentle but firm “I think we want different things” speech in my head.

My mistake was leaving the Iceland brochure at his apartment. I’d been finalizing plans with my travel agent, Sylvia, a lovely woman in her sixties who specialized in solo female travel. I’d shown Mark a photo of a glacier on my phone, mentioned my excitement. He’d gotten quiet. “Alone?” he’d asked, his smile strained. “But… you have me now.” I’d brushed it off, but in my haste to leave after an increasingly awkward evening, the glossy pamphlet with its waterfalls and northern lights stayed tucked between his couch cushions.

His call came the next morning, a volcanic eruption of faux-concern and thinly veiled fury.

“A real girlfriend doesn’t just run off to another country by herself,” he seethed, not even bothering with hello. “What are you trying to prove? That you don’t need me? We’re supposed to be building a future, and you’re planning escapes from it!”

I was stunned. “Mark, we’ve been on three dates. I’m not your girlfriend. And this is my dream, my money—”

“Our money, if we’re together,” he interrupted, his tone switching to a wounded, paternalistic drip. “Sweetheart, I’m just thinking of us. Of your safety. It’s not respectful to me or to what we have.”

I hung up. I fumed. I blocked his number. I considered the speech again, now significantly less gentle. But I had a volcano hike to book, so I pushed him out of my mind.

Until I called Sylvia two days later to confirm a payment.

“Oh, honey,” she said, her voice laced with confusion and pity. “I’m so sorry about everything you’re going through. But I processed the cancellation like you asked. All the deposits have been returned, minus the fees, of course.”

Ice flooded my veins. “Cancellation? Sylvia, I didn’t cancel.”

A slow, horrifying realization unfolded. Someone had called her, impersonating me—a voice “choked with tears,” claiming to be in a mental health crisis, saying the trip was a terrible mistake born of instability. They’d known my booking reference, my dates, even the name of my hotel. They’d sounded so convincingly distraught that Sylvia, bless her heart, had expedited the cancellation as a kindness.

Only one person had that information. Only one person had the brochure.

I unblocked Mark’s number. He answered on the first ring, voice oozing smug concern. “Hey, you. I was hoping you’d call. Are you feeling better?”

“You canceled my trip.” My voice was terrifyingly calm.

A pause. Then, a sigh of profound martyrdom. “I did it for us. For our future. You’ll see. When you’re not being so emotional, you’ll understand it was an act of love. I protected you from a reckless decision. Now we can use that lottery money for something real. A down payment. A wedding fund.”

The audacity was so colossal it circled back to being almost impressive. He’d stolen my dream, framed it as a romantic gesture, and was now budgeting my money. In that moment, any guilt about hurting his feelings evaporated. This wasn’t a misguided suitor; this was a manipulative tyrant playing house with my life.

And that’s when the plan crystallized, cold and sharp as an Icelandic glacier.

“You’re right,” I said, forcing a shaky, contrite note into my voice. “I… I overreacted. It was a lot of money to spend on just me. Maybe… maybe we do have a future.”

I could hear his triumphant inhale on the other end of the line. The “Nice Guy” had won. He’d broken the rebellious mare.

What followed was a month of the most exquisite, purposeful manipulation of my life. I became the girlfriend he wanted: compliant, adoring, future-obsessed. I told him I’d “seen the light” and wanted to use the lottery winnings to build a life “with him.” I let him believe the $50,000 was sitting in a savings account, waiting for his visionary guidance.

And guide me he did. With the fervor of a cult leader, he plunged us into a shared fantasy. He dragged me to open houses for two-bedroom condos we could “totally afford with your winnings.” He spent hours crafting a wedding website with a color scheme I loathed. He picked out china patterns and debated the merits of a hybrid SUV for “when the kids come.” I nodded, smiled, and took notes. Actual, physical notes in a sleek leather folder I’d bought for the purpose.

“This is so amazing, Mark,” I’d gush. “You have such a clear vision for our future.” He ate it up.

Meanwhile, in the shadows, I was on the phone with Sylvia. I told her the whole, ugly story. She was horrified and instantly became my co-conspirator. “Honey, we’re going to get you on a trip so grand it’ll singe his eyebrows off,” she vowed.

With the returned deposits and a significant chunk of the remaining lottery money, we didn’t just rebook Iceland. We upgraded it into a stratospheric, once-in-a-lifetime, non-refundable masterpiece. First-class lie-flat seats to Reykjavik. A private guide for the entire two weeks. A suite at the Retreat at Blue Lagoon, where my own geothermal pool waited. Helicopter tours over erupting volcanoes, exclusive dinners in ice caves, a stay in a luxury bubble hotel under the aurora. It was reckless, it was glorious, and it was for ONE.

The day before my real flight, I met Mark for coffee. I wore the dress he liked. I let him hold my hand.

“I’m so proud of us,” he said, squeezing my fingers. “We’ve built such a beautiful future on paper. Soon, it’ll be real.”

“So soon,” I agreed, smiling. “It’s all I think about.”

The morning of my flight, I packed my suitcase with the warm, expensive gear I’d bought. I placed the leather folder, now bulging with his dream plans—the apartment brochures, the printed wedding website, the SUV specs—on my now-cleared-out coffee table. On top of it, I placed a final item: a fake, meticulously scratched-off lottery ticket I’d ordered online, showing the “Sorry, Not a Winner” message.

Then, I took a car to the airport.

Settled into the serene, hushed sanctuary of the first-class lounge, surrounded by the quiet hum of elegant travelers, I felt a peace deeper than any I’d ever known. I ordered a glass of champagne. I took a picture: the champagne flute, my passport ticket sleeve, and the vast window overlooking the tarmac in the frame. I didn’t show my face.

I sent it to Mark.

The caption read: “Thanks for the life lesson. My future is here. P.S. Check your ‘investment portfolio.’ Blocked.”

I sent it. I blocked his number, his email, his socials. I pictured him, alone in his apartment, receiving the message. The confusion, then the dawning horror. The frantic calls that would go straight to voicemail. The realization that he’d been not just rejected, but meticulously, theatrically outmaneuvered. That the future he’d painstakingly constructed was a prop in my revenge play. That the lottery money was gone, spent on the very solo adventure he’d tried to sabotage. That he’d been left with nothing but a folder of his own delusions and a worthless piece of paper.

As my flight lifted off, climbing through the clouds, the weight of his fantasy finally fell away. Below me was the life he’d tried to cage me in. Ahead was the vast, wild, breathtaking unknown. The champagne bubbles tasted like freedom, and the northern lights were waiting.

The revenge wasn’t just in the luxury he couldn’t touch. It was in the blissful silence he’d never break again. It was in knowing that his “act of love” had funded the very independence it sought to destroy. He’d tried to cancel my vacation, so I upgraded my entire life.
