My Boss Taxed My Lottery Winnings, So I Bought The Company And Made Him My Janitor

Posted on 2026-04-14 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Title: TIFU by buying a lottery ticket on my lunch break, and how it ended with my psycho ex-boss working for me.

Subreddit: r/ProRevenge

Hey everyone. Grab a drink. This is a long one, but I promise the ending is worth every second of the 18-month nightmare that preceded it. It’s about power, a lie, and a dead man’s justice.

My name’s Alex. For three years, I worked at a mid-tier tech firm called NexaLogic. My boss, let’s call him Julian, wasn’t just my manager; he was my “mentor.” At least, that’s what he called himself in every performance review. In reality, he was a control freak with the emotional intelligence of a haunted teapot.

Julian’s “mentorship” involved micromanagement so intense it felt like a psychological experiment. He used time-tracking software that screenshot our monitors every five minutes. He demanded our personal phone passwords “for team transparency and emergency client access.” When I pushed back, he gave me this condescending smile and said, “Alex, paranoia is the enemy of synergy. If you have nothing to hide, you have nothing to fear.” I was 26, it was my first “real” job in the city, and I was terrified of losing it. So, I handed it over.

The lunch breaks were the worst. We had a mandated 30 minutes. Not 29, not 31. He’d literally watch the timestamp on our log-offs. One time, I was 4 minutes late because the elevator broke. He sent me a Slack message that read: “Lunch is a privilege, not a right. A team is only as strong as its most undisciplined member.”

The inciting incident happened on a Tuesday. I was so drained from a morning of Julian breathing down my neck about Q2 projections that I needed air. I clocked out for my 30 and practically ran to the gas station two blocks over. I wanted a disgusting, overpriced energy drink and five minutes of not thinking. On a whim, at the counter, I pointed to the lottery display. “Quick Pick, MegaMillions,” I said, sliding a crumpled five across. The clerk handed me the ticket, a flimsy piece of paper that felt like nothing. I shoved it in my back pocket, gulped my drink, and sprinted back.

I was two minutes early, sweating slightly as I slid into my chair. I must have pulled the ticket out with my phone, because it fluttered to the floor. As I bent to grab it, a shadow fell over me.

Julian.

He plucked the ticket from the floor before my fingers could touch it. He held it up to the fluorescent light, a smirk playing on his lips. “Lottery ticket, Alex? Dreaming of a better life?” His voice was loud enough for the open-plan office to hear. A few heads ducked down.

“Just a quick pick, Julian. For fun,” I mumbled, face burning.

“For fun!” he boomed. “You know, statistically, you’re more likely to be struck by lightning. But hope is a powerful drug.” He tapped the ticket against his palm. “Tell you what. If you win, I’ll consider it a team success. A ‘synergy tax,’ if you will. 10% for morale.” He laughed, a hollow sound. No one else joined in.

“Can I have it back?” I asked, my voice tight.

He looked at the ticket, then at his pristine, empty desk. “You know what? I’m going to hold onto this. Put it right here, in the center of my desk. Let it be a… motivational tool. A reminder to everyone that while we chase pipe dreams, real success comes from disciplined work.” He walked to his glass-walled office, sat down, and carefully centered my ticket next to his nameplate. It was a power move, pure and simple. Humiliating me with my own stupid impulse buy. I spent the rest of the day avoiding the sight of that little slip of paper, a monument to my subjugation.

That night, the numbers were drawn. I didn’t even watch. I was too busy prepping for a presentation Julian would inevitably take credit for.

The next morning, the office was buzzing. Not about work. The MegaMillions jackpot. $273 million. A single winner. Ticket sold at a gas station in our city.

My blood turned to ice. I looked up. The ticket was still there, on Julian’s desk, gleaming under the LED lights.

With trembling hands, I pulled up the winning numbers online. I memorized them. I walked, on numb legs, to the breakroom. From the coffee station, I had a clear view into Julian’s office. He was on the phone, laughing. I focused on the ticket. The numbers. One by one, they matched. Every. Single. One.

The world narrowed to a tunnel. That was my ticket. My quick-pick, bought with my five dollars, during my tyrannically monitored lunch break. The jackpot.

I didn’t knock. I opened his door. He looked up, annoyed, and hung up the phone.

“Julian. The lottery. The ticket. On your desk. I need to check it.”

He followed my gaze to the ticket. His expression didn’t change. It was a flat, calm pond. “What about it, Alex?”

“I think… I think it might be the winner. The jackpot.”

He leaned back, steepling his fingers. “Is that so?” He didn’t even look at it. “That’s interesting. Because I bought a lottery ticket yesterday too. At that same gas station. A quick pick.” He slowly, deliberately, opened his top desk drawer. He pulled out a lottery ticket. It was identical in every way to the one on his desk, save for the numbers. He laid it next to my ticket.

“You see,” he said, his voice dropping to a confidential whisper that felt more threatening than a shout, “the one on the desk is mine. The winning one. I put it there for motivation, remember? You must have seen it and confused it for your own. A easy mistake to make, in your… excited state.”

I was speechless. This wasn’t just theft; it was a theatrical, premeditated takeover. “That’s not true,” I finally choked out. “You took mine. Yesterday. In front of everyone.”

“Did I?” he said, his smirk returning. “Or did you, perhaps, see your mentor with a valuable asset and decide to concoct a story?” He turned his monitor toward me. On it was a grainy video. It showed a timestamp from yesterday, 1:15 PM. It was a security feed, from inside the gas station. It clearly showed Julian, in his signature blue shirt, at the counter, receiving a lottery ticket from the clerk. The time was during my lunch break.

My lunch break, which he knew to the minute.

“I had this pulled for security purposes,” he said smoothly. “Given the value, you understand. I never dreamed I’d need it to prove ownership against my own protégé.”

The floor dropped out from under me. The video was doctored. It had to be. But how? Why was he so prepared? Then it hit me: the phone password. The time-tracking software. He knew exactly when I left, how long it took to walk to that specific gas station. He could have had someone make that video at any time, but he waited until the numbers were drawn to spring the trap. He’d been planning this since the moment he took the ticket, maybe even hoping it would win.

The rising tension was a slow-motion avalanche. He lawyered up immediately—a slick, terrifying man in a $5,000 suit who spoke in quiet legalese that felt like physical blows. I found a lawyer too, a kind but overworked guy named Ben who winced at my story. “You have no receipt?” he asked. I didn’t. It was cash. “Witnesses?” The office was too scared of Julian to back me up. He’d already gathered them for a “team meeting” and, in a voice trembling with “disappointment,” explained how ambition had corrupted poor Alex, how he was trying to steal from the very man who guided him.

He gaslit the entire office. I was a pariah, a cautionary tale. He pulled me into a conference room, the lawyer present, and laid it out. “Drop this, Alex. Sign this NDA admitting your ‘mistake.’ I’ll give you a neutral reference. Pursue it, and I will not rest until you are unemployable in this entire state. I will sue you for defamation, for emotional distress. I will own your future.”

I was fired, of course. For “gross misconduct and attempted fraud.”

My only shred of evidence was a blurry, black-and-white camera still I managed to get from the gas station’s external camera. It showed me, from a distance, at the counter at 1:12 PM. You couldn’t see what I was buying. Julian’s lawyer called it “meaningless.”

I was broke, my reputation in tatters, living on my friend’s couch. The legal battle was a draining, expensive slog going nowhere. Julian, with his doctored video and my office “witnesses,” was winning.

The twist didn’t come from the courtroom. It came from my own desperate, private investigation. I became obsessed with Julian. Who was he? I dug through every piece of public record. I found an old society page photo: a younger Julian with a stern-looking older man at a charity gala. The caption named the man: Alistair Thorne, a reclusive, old-money financier.

Julian’s last name was Walsh. But Thorne… that was his mother’s maiden name. Deeper digging revealed a falling out. Julian had been cut off years ago for reasons unspecified. Alistair Thorne lived in a fortress-like estate 90 minutes north of the city.

I had nothing left to lose. I drove there. I didn’t get past the gate. But I wrote a letter. A long, detailed, unemotional letter. I included copies of the time-tracker logs showing my exact break time, the gas station external image, Julian’s own “team transparency” policy demanding phone passwords, the transcript of his “synergy tax” joke. I outlined the pattern: not just the ticket, but the systemic coercion. I didn’t ask for money. I didn’t mention the lawsuit. I ended it with: “I believe your son learned more than finance from someone. He learned that power is there to be taken. I just thought you should know what he’s building his empire on.”

A week later, my phone rang. A neutral, elderly voice. “Mr. Alex? This is Franklin, Mr. Thorne’s personal assistant. He would like to see you.”

Alistair Thorne was a gaunt, sharp-eyed man in a library that smelled of leather and old paper. He didn’t offer me tea. He gestured to my letter on his desk. “This,” he said, his voice like dry gravel, “is consistent with a pathology I had hoped was merely adolescent avarice. My son has a habit of claiming what is not his. Including, once, a significant portion of funds from a family trust meant for his sister’s medical care. He presented forged documents. He used his mother’s illness as leverage. We haven’t spoken in eight years.”

He was quiet for a long time. “I cannot involve myself in your legal matter. Any intervention from me would only fuel his narrative. But you have shown integrity, and remarkable tenacity, in bringing this to me.” He dismissed me with a nod. I left feeling both seen and hopeless. Nothing had changed.

Then, two months later, Alistair Thorne died suddenly. A stroke.

The news was a small blip in the business section. My lawyer called me, bewildered. “Alex… you need to see this.”

Alistair Thorne’s will was made public. The bulk of his vast estate was left to a suite of charities, as expected. But there was a codicil, added just weeks before his death. It established the “Alexander Grey Integrity Trust.” Named after me.

The language was precise and brutal. It stated that after a lifetime of observing “the corrosive nature of unearned privilege and ethical compromise” in his own family, he wished to endow someone who had demonstrated “fortitude in the face of systemic predation” and “a commitment to truth despite having no power to defend it.” I was named the sole beneficiary and executor. The trust was structured to provide a substantial annual income and, crucially, contained a venture capital fund I was to control.

The final line was a masterpiece: “It is my wish that these resources be used to build a company whose culture is the antithesis of that which destroyed my relationship with my son. And should my son, Julian Walsh, ever find himself in need of employment under fair and ethically governed leadership, I encourage the Trustee to consider him—as a testament to redemption through accountability.”

Julian’s lawsuit against me evaporated overnight. His doctored evidence suddenly looked flimsy against the weight of a dead man’s condemnation. His inheritance was nothing. The legal fees from our battle and his own lavish lifestyle had drained him. The trust fund from his mother was long gone.

Meanwhile, I founded a company. A small, focused tech firm built on the principles Julian violated: transparency, autonomy, respect. We thrived.

A year after the trust was established, I got an email. From Julian Walsh. The subject line: “Inquiry re: Project Manager Position.”

He sat across from me in my office, in a chair that wasn’t glass-walled. He was thinner, the smirk utterly gone, replaced by a tense, desperate attempt at humility. He needed the job. He had a mortgage. A lifestyle. None of the old guard would touch him; his reputation, once his weapon, was now his shackle.

I hired him. Not as a mentor. Not as a boss. As a mid-level project manager, reporting to a team lead half his age. His contract includes strict clauses about data privacy, time management autonomy, and mandatory ethics training. He has no access to passwords. His lunch breaks are not monitored.

He calls me “Mr. Grey.” I call him “Julian.”

The lottery ticket lawsuit was formally dropped as part of his employment agreement. He needed the job more than he needed the lie. Sometimes, I see him looking at the modern art on my wall, or the relaxed, laughing team in the open space, and I see a hollow confusion in his eyes. He built a kingdom on fear and theft, and it turned to sand. I built one on the ashes of my dignity, funded by the conscience of the man he betrayed first.

The revenge isn’t in firing him, or berating him. It’s in the profound, daily normality of his subjugation. It’s in the fact that every single day, he has to walk into a building that is a living monument to his own moral failure, and be grateful for the paycheck that allows him to keep up appearances. It’s in the knowledge that his father, in his final act, looked at us both and chose me.

The ticket? The state gaming commission, after a protracted investigation spurred by the civil suit and the peculiar new circumstances, ultimately voided the claim due to the “irresolvable dispute and evidence of bad faith.” The jackpot rolled over. Somewhere, someone else is dreaming over a quick-pick.

But I don’t need to dream anymore. I just have to walk to my office, past Julian’s meticulously tracked but now irrelevant desk, and close the door on a silence that is finally, blessedly, my own.
