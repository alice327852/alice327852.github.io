My roommate's brother kept using my PS5, so I got revenge via the WiFi network name.

Posted on 2026-04-02 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Subreddit: r/ProRevenge
Post Title: TIFU by telling my roommate’s little brother the WiFi password.

Post:

Let me set the scene: a decent two-bedroom apartment, shared with my roommate Sarah. We’d been friends since college, were both in our mid-twenties grinding through early career hell, and for the most part, it worked. Then there was Ethan.

Ethan was Sarah’s 19-year-old brother. Picture a trust fund kid whose life’s greatest hardship was the 0.5-second latency on his custom gaming PC, bought by mommy and daddy. He’d never been told “no” in any meaningful way. His university was nearby, but he treated our place like his personal lounge/clubhouse. Sarah felt guilty – their parents were “hands-off” (read: paid for everything but emotional presence), so she was the de facto guardian. I was sympathetic. At first.

The WiFi password was my first mistake. A moment of weakness when he’d “needed to submit an assignment.” That password was a digital vampire invitation. He started showing up unannounced, eating my food, drinking my expensive craft beer (replacing it with cheap lager, like I wouldn’t notice), and parking his entitled ass on our couch for hours.

The inciting incident was a Tuesday. I was heading out for my shift at the architecture firm, running late, when Ethan slithered in. He didn’t greet me, just stared at the TV.

“Hey, I’m gonna use your PS5 today. My PC’s updating.”

I stopped, my bag halfway onto my shoulder. “No, you’re not.”

He finally looked at me, annoyed, as if I’d interrupted his royal decree. “Why? You’re not using it.”

“It’s mine. It has all my save files. I don’t have a subscription for you, and I don’t want anyone messing with my games.” Particularly my Baldur’s Gate 3 save. 327 hours. A meticulously crafted saga. My digital comfort zone.

He rolled his eyes. “It’s just a game, dude. I’ll make a guest profile. Don’t be a dick.”

“The answer is no, Ethan.” My tone was final. I left, a knot of irritation in my gut.

The knot tightened throughout my day. I texted Sarah: “Your brother demanded my PS5. I said no. Please back me up.” Her reply was a sigh in text form: “I’ll talk to him. He’s just bored.”

I came home eight hours later, exhausted, dreaming of unwinding with my virtual companions. The sound hit me first: the chaotic gunfire of Call of Duty, not from Ethan’s usual laptop, but from my living room TV. There he was, sprawled on my couch, using my controller, on my PS5. Rage, pure and hot, flooded my veins.

I didn’t scream. I walked calmly to the entertainment unit, reached behind the console, and yanked the power cord from the wall. The screen died mid-killstreak.

“What the fuck, man!” he exploded, jumping up.

“I told you no,” I said, my voice terrifyingly quiet. “Get out. Now.”

“It’s my sister’s apartment too! I have every right to be here!”

“To be here, maybe. To steal my shit, no. Leave.”

A screaming match erupted. He called me a broke loser jealous of his “life.” I called him a spoiled parasite. It was ugly, childish, but the fury was real. Seeing him with his hands on my console, the one thing I’d truly splurged on after landing my job, felt like a profound violation.

I needed air before I did something I’d regret. “I’m going for a walk. When I get back, you better be gone, or I’m calling Sarah and your parents.”

I stormed out, pacing the block for twenty minutes, trying to let the adrenaline fade. When I returned, I inserted my key, turned it… and nothing. The deadbolt was engaged from the inside. He’d locked me out of my own apartment.

Disbelief, then cold, slimy dread. I knocked. “Ethan. Open the door. This isn’t funny.”

Through the narrow gap in the curtains, I could see into the living room. He saw me looking. A nasty, smug smile spread across his face. He walked deliberately to my PS5, now powered back on. I saw my Baldur’s Gate 3 icon on the screen. My heart stopped.

He looked right at me through the window, held my gaze, and then, with a deliberate flick of his finger, he yanked the power cord from the back of the console again. The screen went black.

My blood turned to ice. Corrupting save files… it was the digital equivalent of keying a car, but this car held my photographs, my memories, my work.

My phone buzzed. A text from him: “Should’ve just let me play. You can sleep in your car tonight lol.”

That “lol” did it. The cold dread evaporated, replaced by a focused, crystalline calm. This wasn’t a tantrum anymore. This was war.

First, evidence. I started a screen recording on my phone. I filmed my key failing to turn in the lock. I panned to the window, though he’d now closed the blinds. I stopped the recording, then immediately started another, scrolling from his text message to the recording I’d just taken, narrating clearly: “Today is October 26th. It’s 7:15 PM. My roommate’s brother, Ethan, has illegally locked me out of my apartment after I caught him using my property without permission. He has just texted me this.” I held the phone to the screen.

Then I called the police. Non-emergency line. “I’ve been illegally locked out of my residence by a guest who is not on the lease.” While on hold, I texted Sarah the screenshot of his text and wrote: “Your brother has deadbolted me out. Police are on the way. You need to deal with this NOW.”

The police arrived. Ethan, suddenly playing the confused innocent, claimed it was a “misunderstanding,” that he “thought I was staying elsewhere.” The officers were unimpressed. They made him open the door. I filed a report for illegal eviction. The phrase “illegal eviction” made one cop’s eyebrows rise. It was official now. Paper trail: started.

Sarah came home in the middle of it, horrified. She tore into Ethan, who sulked like a scolded toddler. He was forced to leave. The apologies poured from Sarah: “I’m so sorry, he’s never gone this far, I’ll pay for any damage…”

The damage wasn’t immediately physical. I booted the PS5 with the dread of a surgeon checking for a pulse. It whirred to life. Then I saw it: the corrupted data icon next of my Baldur’s Gate 3 save. The 327-hour masterpiece. Gone. A hollow pit opened in my stomach.

Then I remembered my external portfolio. As a freelance architectural designer, I kept working files, renderings, and client projects on a dedicated external drive… which was often plugged into the PS5 for media viewing. The sudden cut had corrupted several key project files. A week of client work, now glitched and unusable. Lost wages. Professional reputation damage.

Sarah offered me her credit card to buy a new console. “It’s fine, just get a new one.” She didn’t get it. This wasn’t about the hardware. It was about the violation, the intentional destruction, the “lol.”

Small claims court for the value of a PS5 and maybe some estimated lost wages? The maximum was too low for the sheer, premeditated malice. I wanted justice, not reimbursement. I consulted a lawyer, a sharp woman named Clara who specialized in property and tort law. I showed her the police report, the screen recordings, the text.

“He’s a trust fund kid,” I explained. “A fine means nothing. His parents just pay it.”

Clara steepled her fingers. “The trust fund is key. We sue him directly for intentional infliction of property damage, conversion, and negligence resulting in lost wages. We add a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress—the illegal lock-out, the text, the deliberate corruption of your data. It’s aggressive, but the evidence is good.”

“Will it stick?”

“He’s 19. An adult. His parents can’t be directly liable… but his assets can be targeted.”

We filed. The lawsuit asked for $25,000 in damages: value of the console, data recovery services, lost freelance income, and distress.

Ethan’s parents, as predicted, swooped in. Their lawyer offered a settlement: $5,000 and a new PS5. “Let’s put this childishness behind us,” their email read.

Clara responded with a single line: “We are proceeding to court. Discovery will include depositions of Ethan and both of you regarding the source of his funds and his history of behavior.”

The magic word was discovery. We didn’t back down.

A week before the pre-trial hearing, Clara called me, a note of triumph in her voice. “We found a precedent. A similar case where a minor’s trust fund, established by parents but under the child’s control as an adult, was successfully accessed for a tort judgment. The parents can’t shield it. The money is legally his.”

We went to mediation. The smugness was gone from their lawyer. Ethan, for the first time, looked scared. Not scared of me—scared of his parents. His facade of invincibility was cracking.

The judge awarded me $15,000 in damages. It was less than we asked, but the breakdown was beautiful: a significant portion was attributed to the lost wages and the “willful and malicious” damage. The court order specified the funds were to be paid from Ethan’s assets.

His parents, furious, tried to argue. But the trust fund was in his name. The court could garnish it. They had to watch as $12,000 was pulled from his precious fund to pay me. The remaining $3,000 was structured as a payment plan he was responsible for.

The final twist, the poetic justice? His parents, “teaching him responsibility,” cut him off. No more unlimited credit. No more bailing him out. The trust fund was now missing a big, ugly chunk because of his own “lol.”

Last I heard from Sarah (who moved out shortly after, our friendship a casualty of the war), Ethan is working at a Chipotle to make the monthly payments to me. Every month, like clockwork, a $250 check arrives from him. It’s not about the money anymore. It’s the reminder. The taste of consequence.

As for me? I bought a new PS5. I also bought the most expensive, heavy-duty deadbolt on the market. I’m rebuilding my Baldur’s Gate 3 save. It’s not the same, but there’s a certain satisfaction in starting anew, knowing the guy who tried to burn my world down is now sweating over a deep fryer to pay for his tantrum.

Justice isn’t always a thunderclap. Sometimes, it’s a monthly check from a fast-food job, signed by the guy who thought he could lock you out of your own life.
