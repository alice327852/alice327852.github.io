My coworker trashed my custom PS5. I found her secret side-hustle and got her fired.

Posted on 2026-04-29 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Title: She threw away my PS5. So I took everything from her.

Subreddit: r/ProRevenge

Hey everyone. Long-time lurker, first-time poster. I need to share this because I’m still vibrating with a mix of residual anger and sweet, sweet satisfaction. This happened last week. It’s a story about boundaries, pettiness, and why you should never, ever touch another man’s console.

I’m 25, and I’ve been an office assistant at this mid-sized tech firm for three years. It’s a classic story: I do the work of three people, my salary is a joke, but the benefits are decent and, until recently, my coworkers were cool. We’re a group of under-30s who sometimes stick around after hours to blow off steam. We’d order pizza, crack open sodas, and play games on the big conference room screen. It was the one thing that made the soul-crushing grind bearable.

Enter Brittany. Brittany, 23, is the CEO’s daughter. Her title is “Marketing Coordinator,” but her main duties seemed to be curating her Instagram aesthetic and using a company credit card with the limit of a small lottery win. She had her own “office”—really a glorified storage closet they’d renovated with a neon “Girl Boss” sign and a forest of fake plants. She wafted through the office like she owned the place, which, in a familial sense, she did. She’d interrupt real work to ask for help resizing a JPEG for her “vision board,” or she’d loudly complain about the “energy” of the break room.

The incident happened on a Tuesday. We’d planned an after-hours session for the new Gran Turismo update. I brought in my PS5. This wasn’t just any PS5. I’d saved for months for it. I’d customized it with a matte black skin with a subtle, raised geometric pattern—a unique design from an artist on Etsy. It was my pride and joy. After a few races, we called it a night around 10 PM. I was dead tired, and instead of lugging the heavy console back to my car, I tucked it safely under my desk, behind my tower and a small box of printer paper. Out of sight, or so I thought.

The next morning, it was gone.

A cold pit opened in my stomach. I tore my cubicle apart. Nothing. I asked the night janitor, Carlos, a sweet older guy who always saved the good pastries for me. He looked horrified. “The young lady, Brittany,” he said, his voice thick with apology. “Last night, she saw me cleaning. She pointed at your desk. She said, ‘All this outdated tech junk under there needs to go. It’s ruining the clean lines of the workspace.’ She told me to clear it out for the e-waste pickup.”

My blood turned to ice. “E-waste? The industrial dumpster?”

Carlos just nodded, unable to meet my eyes. “She stood there and watched me put it in the bin. I am so sorry, amigo.”

I sprinted to the loading dock. The industrial dumpster, a massive steel beast, had already been serviced. The contents were compacted into a single, dense cube of tragedy. I begged the facilities manager to let me look. There, near the top of the compressed block, was a glimpse of familiar matte black and a flash of that geometric pattern, now grotesquely folded around a mangled desk lamp and what looked like an old keyboard. My PS5. My Gran Turismo disc still inside. My custom controller. All of it, crushed into silicon and plastic confetti.

I didn’t scream. I didn’t cry. I just stood there, a profound, glacial calm settling over me. This wasn’t a mistake. It was an act of casual, privileged vandalism. She saw something that didn’t fit her “aesthetic” and unilaterally sentenced it to destruction. This wasn’t about a game console anymore. This was about a fundamental lack of respect for other people’s property, their space, their lives.

The old me would have stormed into HR. But HR Manager Linda was terrified of Brittany. She’d once made Linda redo her onboarding paperwork three times because the font was “aggressively corporate.” Going to HR would result in a hushed meeting, a non-apology from Brittany, and maybe, maybe, a check for half the console’s value from the “office incident fund.” She’d get a slap on the wrist, and I’d get a lesson in my own insignificance.

No. This demanded a more surgical approach.

I started my campaign that afternoon. Phase One: Evidence.

  1. The Crime Scene: I took detailed, high-resolution photographs of the compacted dumpster, zooming in on the distinctive skin of my console. I got a signed statement from Carlos, detailing exactly what Brittany said and did.
  2. The Smoking Gun: I submitted a formal request to Security for footage from our floor for that night, citing “theft of personal property.” The head of security, a no-nonsense guy named Roy who thought Brittany was a joke, complied without question. The video was crystal clear. There was Brittany, in a ridiculously oversized blazer, pointing imperiously under my desk. You could see her mouthing the words “outdated tech.” You could see her supervising as Carlos reluctantly placed my PS5 into a rolling bin. You could see her giving a satisfied nod as it was wheeled away.
  3. The Damages: I compiled every receipt: the PS5 purchase, the custom skin, the games, the extra controller, the headset. I got official quotes from three separate repair shops, all stating the same thing: “Total loss due to catastrophic physical damage.” The total came to over $850.
  4. The Dossier: This is where it escalated from personal revenge to corporate justice. I began a secondary log. For two weeks, I discreetly tracked Brittany’s “workday.” Her 2-hour “lunch breaks” at the trendy wine bar across the street. Her frequent 3 PM “offsite brainstorming” departures. I even noted the parade of delivery drivers bringing shopping bags to the reception desk for her. One day, from the printer, I saw her monitor was clearly on a luxury consignment website, not a marketing report.

I packaged it all into a single, hyperlinked PDF dossier. It had a table of contents. It had timelines. It had visual evidence. It was professional, damning, and impossible to ignore. The cover page read: “Formal Complaint: Willful Destruction of Private Property & Systematic Misuse of Company Resources.”

I bided my time. The quarterly all-hands meeting was coming up. The CEO, Mr. Henderson, always ended these with a stiff, “Any new business? Anything for the good of the company?” It was usually met with dead silence and the desperate rustling of people trying to leave.

The day arrived. The entire company was crammed into the big auditorium. Brittany was in the front row, looking bored and texting. Mr. Henderson droned through financials. Finally, he sighed. “Any new business?”

My hand went up. A few people turned. I saw Linda from HR tense up. I walked calmly to the central aisle, a USB drive in my hand.

“Yes, sir. I have a matter that impacts both employee morale and company operational efficiency.”

I could feel 200 pairs of eyes on me. I plugged the USB into the main laptop connected to the giant screen. I opened my dossier. The cover page was now visible to the entire company.

“I’d like to present a case study,” I began, my voice steady. I clicked to the security footage. The room watched, in dead silence, as Brittany ordered the disposal of my console. A few muffled gasps. I advanced to the photos of the crushed remains, highlighting my custom skin. I displayed the receipts and the repair quotes.

Then, I switched tabs. “The root cause of this incident appears to be a fundamental misunderstanding of company resources and time.” Up came the log of her extended breaks. I showed a screenshot (blurred for privacy) of a shopping website on a monitor, timestamped against the company clock. I displayed the internal delivery log, highlighting the numerous personal packages.

The room was so quiet you could hear the HVAC hum. Brittany had gone from pale to a blotchy, furious red. She sputtered, “That’s—that’s private! He’s stalking me! Dad, he’s—”

“Quiet, Brittany.” Mr. Henderson’s voice was low, but it cut through the air like a whip. He wasn’t looking at her. He was staring at the screen, his face a mask of cold, building fury. This was the twist I’d hoped for but hadn’t dared to count on.

He stood up and walked slowly to the podium, nudging me aside. He took the clicker.

“This log,” he said, pointing at the screen. “These extended, unapproved absences. The personal use of company logistics.” He turned to face the audience, his gaze sweeping over his stunned employees. “For months, I have been asking why our marketing outreach is lagging. Why our social engagement is down. I was told it was ‘market conditions.’ I was told the team was ‘learning the ropes.’” He turned his icy stare directly at his daughter. “It appears the only ropes being learned were how to tie up delivery packages.”

He wasn’t angry about my PS5. Not directly. He was furious about the brazen inefficiency, the liability, the public embarrassment. He saw a nepo-baby sabotaging his company’s discipline, and in front of everyone, his illusion that she was “learning the business” shattered.

“Linda,” he barked. HR Manager Linda jumped as if electrocuted. “Escort Brittany to her office. She is to collect personal items only. Security will supervise. Her company access is revoked as of this moment. Cancel her corporate card.”

Brittany was crying now, ugly, heaving sobs. “Daddy, please! It was just an old box! He left junk—”

“It was not your property!” he roared, finally losing his composure. “You do not get to decide what is ‘junk’ in this office! You have abused your position and wasted company time and money. Get out of my sight.”

Two security guards appeared at the aisle. They didn’t touch her, but their presence was absolute. Sniffling, humiliated, she was led out of the auditorium, past a sea of her former colleagues who refused to meet her eyes. The silence after the door closed was profound.

Mr. Henderson took a deep breath. He turned to me. “You will be fully reimbursed for your property by the end of today. Submit the paperwork directly to my office. Furthermore,” he said, addressing the room again, “for bringing this… operational inefficiency to light in such a thorough manner, you will receive a separate bonus. Consider it a reward for improving company transparency.”

The meeting ended in a surreal murmur. Back at my desk, by 3 PM, an inter-departmental envelope arrived. It contained a check for the full amount of my damages, plus a separate check for a $2,000 “Discretionary Performance Bonus.” At 4 PM, I watched from my window as Brittany’s “Girl Boss” neon sign was unceremoniously dumped into a regular trash can.

Epilogue: I ordered a PS5 Pro that night. It’s sitting under my TV, not my desk. The custom skin is a different design. I needed a fresh start.

As for Brittany? Rumor has it her dad pulled some strings. She’s now an “unpaid intern” in the mailroom of his biggest rival. Apparently, he told his friend over there, “Make her earn every stamp. No special treatment.”

The schadenfreude is immense. Carlos gets a coffee on me every Friday. And the after-hours gaming sessions are back on, with a strict “take your console home” rule. The energy in the office is lighter. People actually work now.

Sometimes, the best revenge isn’t screaming. It’s a well-formatted PDF presented at the right time, to the right audience.

TL;DR: Nepo-baby threw my custom PS5 in the compactor because it messed with her vibe. I documented her crime and her chronic laziness, and presented it to her CEO dad in front of the whole company. She got fired, I got paid, and she’s now licking envelopes at a rival firm.
