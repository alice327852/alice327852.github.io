My boyfriend smashed my gaming PC, so I sold his dream concert ticket to rebuild it.

Posted on 2026-04-27 In Reddit Stories

🎬 Video Hook

Title: He destroyed my gaming PC. He forgot about the ticket taped under the keyboard.
Subreddit: r/ProRevenge

It all started with a notification. Not a dramatic, caught-in-the-act moment, but a stupid, bright bubble on a locked screen. I was deep into a Baldur’s Gate 3 session, my custom-built PC humming its familiar, comforting song. Mark’s phone, charging on the couch beside me, lit up. I glanced over, annoyed at the distraction.

The preview text was unmistakable: “Can’t wait for tomorrow night. My place? I’ll wear what you like.” Sent from a contact saved as “Jen - Work.”

The floor dropped out from under me. The vibrant colors of my game blurred into a nauseating swirl. I felt cold, then hot, then numb. For ten minutes, I just stared at my paused game, my character standing idle in the Shadow-Cursed Lands, which suddenly felt less dark than my own living room.

When Mark got home, I was still at my desk, the game shut down. I asked him, voice eerily calm, about “Jen from Work.” He did the dance. Denial. Deflection. “Just a flirty coworker, it means nothing, you’re overreacting, you’re always glued to that screen anyway.”

That last one stung, because it was his go-to. My gaming was my hobby, my sanctuary. I worked as a graphic designer from home; this PC was my office, my studio, my escape. He’d always resented it, calling it my “other husband.” Turns out, he was projecting.

The fight erupted, volcanic and toxic. He screamed about neglect. I screamed about betrayal. He never once gave a full confession, just slippery, gaslighting non-apologies. “I needed attention! You give more to your digital friends than to me!”

Exhausted and feeling like I was drowning, I did the mature thing. “I need space,” I said, my voice ragged. “I’m going for a drive. We’ll talk when you can be honest.” I left him sulking on the couch, looking more pissed than remorseful.

I drove for two hours, listening to angry music and crying until my eyes were sandpaper. I called my best friend, Sarah, who validated every furious, broken feeling. I started to steel myself. Maybe we could get counseling. Maybe, somehow, we could fix this. The house was mine—inherited from my grandmother—but it had been our home for five years.

I returned after dark, hoping the silence would mean he was asleep, that we could have a cold truce until morning.

I was wrong.

The first thing I saw was the light from my office spilling into the hall. The second thing was the sound of glass crunching under my shoe.

My heart stopped.

There, on the floor of my office, was the corpse of my PC. My beautiful, painstakingly assembled machine. He hadn’t just unplugged it. He’d clearly yanked it off the desk in a rage. The tempered glass side panel was a spiderweb of cracks. My GPU—a powerful RTX model I’d saved for months to buy—was bent at a sickening angle, ripped partly from its slot. Wires were torn, and my CPU cooler hung limply. Fragments of my favorite mug, which had been on the desk, were scattered among the components like shrapnel.

I stood in the doorway, hand over my mouth, not breathing. This wasn’t just a fit of anger. This was a targeted annihilation. He’d destroyed my work, my passion, my property. The violation was profound, somehow even deeper than the cheating.

Then I heard his voice from the bedroom doorway. “Now maybe you’ll see what’s important.”

He said it with such smug, righteous finality. He wasn’t sorry. He was proud.

I didn’t say a word. I looked from the wreckage to his face, a mask of petulant triumph, and something in me solidified. The grief and betrayal hardened into a diamond core of cold, clear purpose. Counseling was off the table. Forgiveness was impossible. He had declared war on my peace. Now he’d face the consequences.

I turned, walked into the guest bedroom, and locked the door. I didn’t sleep. I plotted.

The next morning, I waited until he left for work. He slammed the front door, the house vibrating with his lingering spite. As soon as his car was gone, I moved.

First, I went to my office. Kneeling in the debris, my hands trembling, I carefully lifted the corpse of my keyboard. It was a heavy, mechanical thing. With a surgeon’s care, I turned it over.

And there it was.

A simple, unassuming $5 scratch-off lottery ticket, protected by a layer of clear packing tape. It was faded a little, the corners worn. My brother, Leo, had given it to me for luck the week before he passed away from cancer four years prior. We’d scratched it together in his hospital room. It was a $10,000 winner.

He’d been so thrilled. “A sign!” he’d wheezed, his smile brighter than I’d seen in months. “That’s for your dream trip, Jess. Go see the Northern Lights. Live for both of us.”

I’d never cashed it. It was my lucky charm, my tangible connection to him. I’d taped it under my keyboard, a secret reservoir of hope. Mark knew I kept a “lucky ticket” from Leo there, but he’d never asked for details, never cared enough to look. He’d dismissed it as sentimental junk. I’d been saving it, quietly adding to the fund, dreaming of surprising Mark with an epic anniversary trip to Iceland. A second honeymoon to rekindle what I thought was just a fading spark.

Now, staring at it amidst the ruins he’d made of my life, I felt Leo’s presence. Not as sadness, but as a fierce, approving strength. Use it, he seemed to whisper.

I carefully peeled the tape back and slid the ticket out. It was still valid. Still a winner.

The revenge, suddenly, had a beautiful, poetic shape.

Phase One: Fortress. I called a locksmith and had every exterior lock changed within the hour. I called a lawyer next, one recommended by Sarah who specialized in tenancy and property law. I explained the situation: sole homeowner, live-in spouse destroying significant personal property, creating a hostile and unsafe environment. She told me that in our state, this constituted “malicious and willful destruction” of my property, which gave me grounds for an immediate eviction notice for cause. She emailed me the official forms.

Phase Two: Purge. I didn’t pack his things with care. I used black contractor garbage bags. I folded nothing. His clothes, his gadgets, his cherished vintage sneakers—everything went in like landfill. His precious signed baseball? Tossed in a bag with his gym socks. I was meticulous, though. I wanted every last trace of him out. I piled the bags in the garage, a monument to his new status: trash.

Phase Three: The Blade. I filled out the eviction notice, citing the specific incident and the dollar value of the destroyed property (a rough estimate of $2,000 for the PC). I taped it to the front door, legally served. Then, I sat at the kitchen table with the winning lottery ticket and my phone.

I took a picture of the ticket, the prize amount clearly visible. I opened my text thread with Mark. The last message from him was from the night before, after I’d locked myself away: “You can’t ignore this forever. We need to talk.”

I attached the photo. My thumbs hovered over the screen. The fury, the heartbreak, the calculated coldness all distilled into a few perfect sentences. I typed:

“My PC was worth $2k. The vacation you just lost was worth 10k. Find a new home.”

I hit send. Then I blocked his number.

The silence that followed was the most satisfying sound I’d ever heard. It was the sound of freedom.

The aftermath was a symphony of schadenfreuge. My phone, through which Sarah was acting as a communications buffer, blew up. He called her screaming, crying, threatening. He claimed he had tenant rights (he didn’t, not against a cause like this). He said he’d sue me for his things (he could try; they were unharmed, just bagged). He begged her to tell me it was a mistake, that he was sorry.

Sarah relayed it all with gleeful commentary. “He’s currently comparing you to a storybook villain,” she laughed. “He says you ‘set him up.’”

A few days later, I cashed the ticket. I took a photo of the bank check and sent it to Sarah with the caption: “Leo sends his regards.”

I used part of the money to hire the lawyer to handle any nonsense, which she did with brutal efficiency. He tried to contest the eviction, but the police report I’d filed for the destroyed property, coupled with photos of the damage, made it an open-and-shut case. The other woman, “Jen from Work,” apparently wanted nothing to do with a homeless, raging cheater. Last I heard, he was couch-surfing with a reluctant friend from his gym.

The rest of the money? I built a new PC. A beast of a machine. An RTX 4090, liquid cooling, the works. I mounted Leo’s ticket, now framed, on the wall above it. I also booked a trip. Not to Iceland. To Japan, for a gaming convention and a tour of the cherry blossoms. I’m going with Sarah next spring.

Sometimes, when the new PC purrs to life, its RGB lights casting a soft glow on Leo’s ticket, I think about that shattered PC on the floor. I don’t feel anger anymore. I feel gratitude. In his vicious, childish tantrum, Mark didn’t just break my computer. He broke the final illusion I had of him, of us. He freed me. And in doing so, he unlocked the winning ticket he never knew was there—both the paper one that paid for my future, and the metaphorical one that got his toxic ass out of my life for good.

Pro revenge isn’t always about ruining someone’s life. Sometimes, it’s about them accidentally handing you the tools to rebuild your own, infinitely better, right in front of their oblivious eyes. And then changing the locks while they’re at work.
