My niece sold my emotional support lizard's entire habitat to buy a PlayStation.

Posted on 2026-04-21 In Reddit Stories

Subreddit: r/ProRevenge
Title: TIFU by trusting my niece and now she’ll never own a PS5

They say you should never attribute to malice what can be explained by stupidity. But what about a malice so profound, so casually executed, that it feels like a physical blow? That’s Chloe. My 16-year-old niece. The golden child of my sister and her husband, a kid who’d never been told “no” unless it was immediately followed by “but here’s a better yes.”

I should have known. I did know. Everyone in the family knew Chloe’s “borrowing” habit. A pair of diamond earrings from Grandma, mysteriously “lost.” My cousin’s vintage concert tee, sold on Depop because she “didn’t think he’d miss it.” Her parents’ solution was always a shrug, a check to cover the cost, and a muttered, “She’s just spirited.” Her latest obsession was a PlayStation 5. She talked about it constantly. Her parents, for once, tried to instill a shred of patience. “Wait for your birthday,” they said. That was four months away. An eternity for a kid like Chloe.

My weak spot, my whole heart, was Smaug. A ten-year-old bearded dragon, the color of sandstone and desert sunsets. I got him during a deep, dark period of my life—crippling anxiety, agoraphobia. He was my routine, my calm. The simple act of feeding him, watching him bask, his slow, dignified blinks… it grounded me. My therapist officially designated him as an Emotional Support Animal. He wasn’t just a pet; he was a key part of my stability.

My job required a two-week trip to oversee a project launch. Panic immediately set in. Who would care for Smaug? My sister’s family lived twenty minutes away. Chloe, smelling an opportunity, pounced. “I’ll do it, Uncle Mark! I love animals!” she chirped, her smile a perfect, practiced thing. My sister beamed. “See? She’s responsible!” My gut screamed no, but the logistical part of my brain argued. They were family. It was just feeding and lights. I wrote out three pages of instructions, showed her everything twice, and left feeling uneasy but hopeful.

The project was a nightmare. I was exhausted, counting the hours until I could get home to my quiet apartment and my reptilian buddy. I pushed open the door, dropping my suitcase with a sigh of relief. “Hey, Smaugy,” I called softly.

The silence was wrong. No usual scratching against the glass of his 75-gallon terrarium. The living room was dim, but the tank lights were off. My heart started a slow, sickening thud. I flicked on the overhead light.

The terrarium was empty. Not just Smaug-missing empty. Stripped bare. His favorite basking rock, his hand-carved hide, his water dish—all gone. The tank stood there like a gaping, dusty mouth. A cold void opened in my chest.

Then I saw it.

On my coffee table, dwarfing everything else, was a box. A big, pristine, white box with the unmistakable logo: PlayStation 5. Digital Edition.

My brain short-circuited. The PS5 retailed for $399.99.

I stumbled to the tank, my hands trembling. Maybe… maybe Chloe had moved him for cleaning? A desperate, stupid hope. I checked my bedroom, the bathroom. Nothing. I pulled out my phone, my fingers slipping on the screen. I opened the Craigslist app, my breath coming in short gasps. I typed “bearded dragon” and filtered for the last two weeks.

There he was. My Smaug. A photo taken right here in my living room, his terrarium in the background. The listing read: “Healthy adult bearded dragon, full setup. Need gone ASAP. $400 OBO.” The price wasn’t an OBO. It was exact. The listing was marked “SOLD.”

The timeline crystallized with horrifying clarity. She’d posted him the day after I left. Sold him and his entire home, my carefully curated setup, for the exact price of the shiny toy now sitting in my living room. She’d used my home, my trust, and my pet as her personal ATM.

A white-hot rage, colder than ice and sharper than glass, settled over me. I didn’t scream. I didn’t cry. I took photos. Of the empty tank. Of the PS5 box, its receipt tucked under a corner, clearly showing Chloe’s name and her debit card transaction. She’d left it here, I realized, as a “temporary” measure. Probably didn’t want her parents to see it until the “right” time.

I called my sister. My voice was terrifyingly calm. “You need to come over. Now. All of you.”

They arrived forty minutes later. Chloe had the audacity to look bored. My sister was annoyed. “Mark, what’s so urgent? We were about to have dinner.”

I pointed to the terrarium. “Where is Smaug?”

Chloe’s eyes flickered to the empty tank, then to the PS5 box. A smirk played on her lips. My sister frowned. “What do you mean? Isn’t he in his tank?”

“No,” I said, my gaze locked on Chloe. “He’s not. Because Chloe sold him. For this.” I pointed at the PlayStation.

My brother-in-law laughed, a dismissive snort. “Don’t be absurd, Mark.”

Chloe, emboldened by her father’s disbelief, shrugged. “It’s just a stupid lizard. I got something better. You should be happy for me.”

That sentence, the sheer, unadulterated cruelty of it, sealed everything. My sister gasped. “Chloe! Did you…?”

“He was my property,” I said, the calm in my voice fracturing slightly. “He was microchipped. He was my Emotional Support Animal. You didn’t just sell a pet, Chloe. You committed theft.”

Her parents launched into a frantic damage-control mode. “We’ll pay you for the lizard! For God’s sake, Mark, it’s a reptile! We’ll get you a new one!”

“You don’t get it,” I said, picking up the PS5 box. “This isn’t a negotiation. Get out of my house.”

I didn’t sleep that night. I sat in the dark, and I planned. The fury had cooled into a solid, determined core. I would not yell. I would not negotiate. I would break them, legally and financially, as they had broken something sacred in me.

First thing in the morning, I called a lawyer—a pit bull of a woman named Amanda who specialized in property and civil litigation. I showed her everything: the microchip paperwork, Smaug’s ESA letter from my therapist, my therapy bills, the Craigslist screenshot, the photos of the PS5 receipt. She listened, her eyes gleaming with cold, professional fury. “We’re going to make an example of them,” she said.

We filed a police report for theft. Having the microchip and the Craigslist ad with the photo made it an open-and-shut case for the cops. Chloe was issued a citation.

Then we sued. We sued Chloe for theft and conversion of property. We sued her parents, as she was a minor, for negligent supervision. And we went big. We sued for the value of Smaug (not just a bearded dragon, but a mature, documented ESA), for emotional distress (citing my documented anxiety spike and additional therapy sessions), and for punitive damages—a legal punishment for the intentional, cruel nature of the act.

The lawsuit was the atom bomb that shattered their carefully curated world. My sister called, crying, screaming that I was destroying the family. “You’re choosing a lizard over your own blood!” she wailed.

“Chloe chose a game console over her family,” I replied flatly. “You raised her to believe that’s acceptable. Now you face the consequences.”

Their lawyer advised them to settle. The evidence was too damning. Going to court would mean a public record of Chloe’s actions, a possible judgment against them, and my lawyer’s fees, which were mounting.

The settlement negotiation was brutal. Amanda was a maestro. The final terms were a work of art:

  1. They covered all my legal fees.
  2. They paid a five-figure financial settlement (the exact number is covered by the NDA, but let’s say it was more than my car is worth).
  3. Chloe had to deliver a court-ordered, written apology to me, admitting her guilt in specific, unflinching detail.
  4. And the masterpiece, Amanda’s pièce de résistance: A clause stating that until Chloe turns 25 years old, neither she nor her parents, nor any agent acting on their behalf, shall purchase, lease, receive as a gift, or otherwise acquire or possess a PlayStation 5 console of any model. Any funds gifted to Chloe for the purpose of buying one, or any cash equivalents up to the console’s value, would be held in a trust I controlled until her 25th birthday. If they violated this, the full, original lawsuit amount would become immediately payable.

They signed it. They had no choice.

The apology letter arrived. It was clearly drafted by their lawyer, but Chloe had to copy it in her own handwriting. “I am sorry for stealing and selling Smaug. It was cruel and wrong. I understand now that pets are not disposable objects…” Reading it gave me no joy, only a hollow sense of closure.

The PS5 sat in my closet, a tombstone for my trust. I finally took it out one weekend, listed it for a fair price, and sold it to a very excited teenager. His joy was genuine, untainted.

With that money, combined with a portion of the settlement, I did two things. I went to a reputable breeder and brought home a young, bright-eyed bearded dragon. I named him Bard. And I established the “Smaug Memorial Fund,” a donation to a local reptile rescue that covers medical care for abandoned bearded dragons. Bard’s terrarium is in a different part of the living room now. I couldn’t bear to look at the old spot.

I hear through the family grapevine that Chloe is “miserable” and “being bullied by her uncle.” My sister and I don’t speak. Her parents had to take out a second mortgage to cover the settlement and their own legal costs. Chloe’s sweet sixteen party was canceled. Every time a new PS5 game or ad comes out, she’s reminded of what she did. She’s reminded that her actions have a name, a price, and a nine-year prohibition.

People might think the revenge was the money, or the legal victory. It’s not. The revenge is in the ban. It’s in the knowledge that for nearly a decade, every time she sees that sleek white console, every time her friends talk about the latest exclusive, she is barred from it. Not by price, not by scarcity, but by a court-enforced consequence stemming directly from her own cruelty. She sold my dragon for a PS5. Now, she’ll never own one.

And Bard, sitting on my shoulder as I type this, gives a slow, contented blink. The rescue in Smaug’s name has already helped over a dozen “stupid lizards” find loving homes. That feels like a better legacy than any game console could ever be.
